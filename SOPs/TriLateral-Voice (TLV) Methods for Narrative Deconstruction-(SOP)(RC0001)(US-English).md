## Executive Summary

The Methods for the Deconstruction of Presenting Narratives is a Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) for systematically dismantling any narrative — spoken, written, broadcast, institutional, or interpersonal — to surface its hidden architecture: the assumptions it rests on, the framings it deploys, the evidence it selects and omits, the emotional levers it pulls, and the power structures it serves.

**Core Principle:** Every presenting narrative is a construction. It selects, sequences, frames, emphasizes, and omits. Deconstruction reverses that process — working backward from the finished narrative to reveal the choices that built it, the assumptions those choices rest on, and the interests those assumptions serve.

**Why this matters:** Narratives are how humans organize reality. They determine what counts as a problem, who counts as a villain or hero, what counts as a solution, and what gets ignored entirely. A narrative that goes undeconstructed operates as invisible architecture — shaping perception, constraining possibility, and directing action without the perceiver's awareness or consent.

This is the white marble problem applied to stories. Just as scholars saw unpainted marble and projected aesthetic theory onto it, we receive constructed narratives and mistake them for reality. We build our understanding — and our decisions, institutions, policies, relationships — on the narrative as presented, never asking: Who built this story? What did they leave out? What assumptions does it require me to accept? What would this look like if told from a different angle?

**This SOP is a convergence product.** It emerges from the intersection of:
- **SOP-IOT** (Methods for the Interrogation of Thought) — the seven-phase cognitive quality assurance framework that provides the assumption-surfacing and falsifiability-testing engine
- **SOP-IM** (Interrogation Methods) — the six micro-techniques (Descent, Contradiction, Reframe, Pattern, Counterfactual, Embodiment) that provide the scalpel for cutting through layers of meaning
- **SOP-SP** (Schema Priming) — the cognitive map-building method that ensures deconstruction is systematic rather than reactive
- **SOP-ER** (Effective Research) — the schema-integrated research methodology that provides the evidentiary backbone: source tracing, reliability tiering, citation chains, and gap flagging
- **SOP-SI** (Self-Interrogation) — the solo reflection methods that surface the deconstructor's own biases, projections, and emotional reactions to the narrative
- **SOP-CI** (Collaborative Interrogation) — the team methods that enable multi-perspective deconstruction, preventing any single analyst's blind spots from dominating

Where previous SOPs ask "How do I think clearly?" (SOP-IOT), "How do I research rigorously?" (SOP-ER), and "How do I interrogate my own assumptions?" (SOP-SI), this SOP asks: **"How do I take apart someone else's story to see what it's really doing?"**

**How this SOP relates to the ecosystem:**

| SOP | Relationship to SOP-DN |
|---|---|
| **SOP-IOT** | Provides the assumption-mapping and falsifiability-testing engine used in Phases 3–5 |
| **SOP-IM** | Provides the six micro-methods deployed as the analytical scalpel throughout all phases |

| **SOP-ER** | Provides the research methodology used in Phase 4 to verify/falsify narrative claims against the evidentiary record |

| **SOP-CI** | Provides the team methods used when deconstruction is multi-analyst |

## Mission

To provide practitioners with a systematic, layered methodology for deconstructing any presenting narrative — surfacing its hidden assumptions, identifying its rhetorical strategies, testing its evidentiary claims, mapping its omissions, and documenting the interests it serves — whether the narrative appears in media, institutional communications, interpersonal discourse, political rhetoric, marketing, legal argument, or any other domain where stories shape reality.

## Core Understanding

### The Painted Narrative Problem

Every narrative you encounter has been painted. It arrives with color, shading, composition, and frame — all chosen by the narrator. Your job as a deconstructor is not to see the paint and call it marble. Your job is to identify the paint, map the choices, and ask: what does this statue look like underneath?

The cognitive cascade that the Interrogation SOP (SOP-IOT) identifies operates with particular force in narratives:

1. **We receive a narrative** — always partial, always constructed, always serving someone's interests
2. **We fill gaps with assumptions** — the narrative provides a frame, and our brain fills in what the frame implies
3. **We mistake the narrative for reality** — the story becomes "what happened" rather than "one account of what happened"
4. **We build understanding on the narrative** — decisions, judgments, alliances, policies flow from the story as received
5. **We rationalize when contradictions emerge** — when evidence challenges the narrative, we defend the story rather than revise it

The cost: We act on stories we never examined. We judge people based on narratives constructed to make us judge them. We support policies designed to benefit the narrator. We oppose things we've never independently evaluated. We become instruments of someone else's framing.

Narrative deconstruction breaks this cascade by working backward: starting with the finished narrative and systematically dismantling it to reveal its architecture, its assumptions, its evidence (and missing evidence), its rhetorical techniques, and its interests.

**This is not cynicism.** Not all narratives are malicious. Some are well-intentioned but flawed. Some are honest but incomplete. Some are sophisticated propaganda. Deconstruction doesn't assume bad faith — it surfaces structure. What you do with that structural awareness is a separate decision.

**This is not nihilism.** Deconstruction doesn't claim that no narrative is true or that all stories are equally valid. It provides the tools to distinguish well-constructed, evidence-supported narratives from poorly-constructed, evidence-free, or deliberately manipulative ones. The goal is not to disbelieve everything — it is to believe nothing without examination.

## Part One: Schema and Preparation (SOP-SP + SOP-ER Phase 2)

### 1.1 Before You Cut, Map the Territory

Deconstruction without schema is reactive — you notice whatever catches your attention, miss structural patterns, and project your own biases onto the narrative. Schema Priming prevents this by building the cognitive map before the analytical scalpel comes out.

**Invoke SOP-SP Methods 1–3 (Syntopical Scoping, Smart Skimming, Detail Coding):**

Process:
1. **Identify the narrative** — What specific narrative are you deconstructing? A speech? An article? A documentary? A corporate communication? A person's account of events? Name it precisely.
2. **Identify the narrator** — Who is telling this story? What is their position, their authority, their relationship to the subject? What is known about their track record, biases, and incentives?
3. **Identify the context** — When was this narrative produced? For whom? In response to what? What was happening in the world/industry/relationship at the time?
4. **Scope the landscape** — What other narratives exist about the same subject? Who else has told this story differently? What competing accounts, counter-narratives, or independent reporting exists?
5. **Initialize the Deconstruction Ledger** (Type D) — a structured document that will track every finding across all phases (see Appendix B for template)

**Invoke SOP-SP Methods 4–5 (Hypothesis Mapping, Gap Flagging):**

6. **Form initial hypotheses** — Based on scoping, what do you think this narrative is doing? What is it trying to accomplish? What does it assume you'll accept without question?
7. **Flag initial gaps** — What perspectives are already visibly absent? What questions does the scoping raise that the narrative doesn't address?

### 1.2 The Deconstruction Ledger (Type D)

The Deconstruction Ledger is the SOP-DN equivalent of the Resource Directory (Type S) from SOP-ER. It is the single source of truth for the entire deconstruction, initialized in Phase 1 and updated in every subsequent phase.

| Section | Content | Updated In |
|---|---|---|
| D.1 Narrative Registry | The narrative(s) being deconstructed — source, narrator, date, format, audience | Phase 1 |
| D.2 Claim Register | Every factual claim extracted from the narrative, with verification status | Phases 2, 5 |
| D.3 Assumption Map | Every assumption the narrative requires the audience to accept | Phase 4 |
| D.4 Rhetorical Technique Log | Every rhetorical, emotional, or persuasive technique identified | Phase 6 |
| D.5 Omission Register | Everything the narrative doesn't say that it could or should | Phase 7 |
| D.6 Interest Map | Who benefits from this narrative being accepted as presented | Phase 8 |
| D.7 Evidence Audit Trail | Source verification results, reliability tiers, citation traces | Phase 5 |
| D.8 Counter-Narrative Log | Alternative framings, accounts, and perspectives | Phase 9 |
| D.9 Analyst Bias Log | The deconstructor's own biases, reactions, and blind spots surfaced during analysis | Phase 10 |

Interrogation Checkpoint (SOP-IOT Phase 1):
- Have I identified the narrative precisely, or am I deconstructing a vague impression?
- Do I know who the narrator is and what their position/incentives are?
- Have I scoped the landscape widely enough to know what competing narratives exist?
- Am I approaching this deconstruction with genuine curiosity, or have I already decided the narrative is wrong?

## Part Two: Surface Capture

### 2.1 Purpose

Capture the narrative exactly as presented — verbatim where possible — before any analysis begins. This is the TLV Transcription Voice applied to narrative: what was actually said, shown, written, or communicated?

### 2.2 Process

1. **Record the narrative verbatim** — If text, copy the full text. If speech, transcribe word-for-word. If video/multimedia, transcribe the spoken content AND note visual choices (camera angles, editing cuts, music, graphics, imagery).
2. **Preserve the medium** — Note the format: written article, video, speech, social media post, legal filing, internal memo, conversation. The medium shapes what's possible and what's implied.
3. **Note structural choices** — How is the narrative organized? What comes first? What comes last? What gets the most space/time? What gets compressed or mentioned only briefly?
4. **Identify all claims** — Extract every factual claim (things presented as true) and log them in D.2 (Claim Register) with a status of "UNVERIFIED."
5. **Identify all characterizations** — How does the narrative characterize people, groups, or entities? What adjectives, metaphors, and descriptors are used? Log in D.4 (Rhetorical Technique Log).

### 2.3 The Separation Rule

During Surface Capture, you are documenting — not analyzing. Keep Transcription Voice (what was said) completely separate from Observation Voice (how it was said) and Interpretation Voice (what it means). Analysis begins in Phase 3.

Interrogation Checkpoint (SOP-IOT Phase 1):
- Am I capturing what was actually said, or already inserting my interpretation?
- Have I noted the medium, format, and structural choices — not just the content?
- Have I extracted every factual claim, or only the ones that seem important?

## Part Three: Layer Separation

### 3.1 Purpose

Separate the narrative into its constituent layers — what happened, how it was told, and why it was told that way. This is the three-layer model from SOP-IM applied to the presenting narrative.

### 3.2 The Three Layers

**Layer 1 — Semantic (What):**
What does the narrative literally claim? What events does it describe? What facts does it assert? What arguments does it make? Strip away all framing, tone, and rhetorical technique — what are the bare propositional claims?

**Layer 2 — Pragmatic (How):**
How does the narrative deliver its claims? What rhetorical structure does it use? What sequence, emphasis, tone, pacing, and stylistic choices shape the delivery? What does the *form* of the narrative do that the content alone doesn't?

This layer includes:
- **Sequencing** — What comes first creates the lens through which everything after is interpreted (primacy effect)
- **Emphasis** — What gets the most attention, space, or repetitive reinforcement
- **Tone** — Authoritative? Emotional? Casual? Urgent? Clinical?
- **Pacing** — What's lingered on vs. rushed past
- **Visual/auditory choices** (if multimedia) — Camera angles, music, editing, graphics

**Layer 3 — Psychological (Why):**
Why was this narrative constructed this way? What does the narrator want the audience to think, feel, believe, or do? What emotional response is being engineered? What worldview does the narrative assume and reinforce?

This layer includes:
- **Narrator intent** — What is the narrative trying to accomplish?
- **Audience engineering** — What emotional, cognitive, or behavioral response is being sought?
- **Worldview reinforcement** — What does the audience need to already believe for this narrative to land?
- **Power function** — Whose interests are served by this narrative being accepted?

### 3.3 Apply SOP-IM Method 1 (The Descent) to the Full Narrative

- **What** does this narrative say? (Semantic — extract all claims)
- **How** does it say it? (Pragmatic — map all delivery choices)
- **Why** does it say it that way? (Psychological — hypothesize intent, engineering, and function)

Log Layer 2 findings in D.4 (Rhetorical Technique Log). Log Layer 3 hypotheses in D.6 (Interest Map) as provisional.

Interrogation Checkpoint (SOP-IOT Phase 2):
- Am I keeping the three layers genuinely separate, or collapsing "how it was said" into "what it means"?
- Am I treating my Layer 3 (psychological) readings as hypotheses, not conclusions?
- What assumptions am I making about the narrator's intent? Are those assumptions based on evidence or projection?

## Part Four: Assumption Excavation

### 4.1 Purpose

Surface every assumption the narrative requires the audience to accept — consciously or unconsciously — for the narrative to function as presented. This is the core of deconstruction: finding the invisible load-bearing walls.

### 4.2 Process

**Step 1 — Identify Explicit Assumptions:**
What does the narrative openly ask you to accept? These are the stated premises: "Given that X is true..." or "Everyone knows that..."

**Step 2 — Identify Implicit Assumptions:**
What does the narrative require you to accept without saying so? These are the unstated premises — the things that have to be true for the narrative's logic to hold, but which are never articulated or defended.

Apply SOP-IOT Phase 2 (Map Underlying Assumptions):
- What has to be true for this narrative to be correct?
- What is being taken for granted?
- What seems obvious but isn't stated?
- What beliefs about people, institutions, or the world is the narrative invoking without defending?

**Step 3 — Categorize Assumptions:**

| Category | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Empirical** | Factual claims that can be tested | "Crime is rising" |
| **Conceptual** | Definitional — how terms are used | "Freedom means deregulation" |
| **Causal** | Claims about cause and effect | "Immigration causes unemployment" |
| **Psychological** | Claims about human motivation and behavior | "People are inherently selfish" |
| **Epistemological** | Claims about how we know things | "Science is the only reliable knowledge" |
| **Moral** | Claims about what is right/good/just | "Market efficiency is a moral good" |
| **Ontological** | Claims about what exists or is real | "There are only two sides to this issue" |

**Step 4 — Create the Assumption Dependency Map:**
Which assumptions support the central narrative? Which assumptions depend on other assumptions? What's the foundation? If the foundational assumption fails, what collapses with it?

Log all assumptions in D.3 (Assumption Map) with category, dependency relationships, and initial assessment (Stated/Unstated, Tested/Untested, Shared/Idiosyncratic).

### 4.3 Apply SOP-IM Method 5 (The Counterfactual)

For each key assumption, ask:
- The narrative assumes X. What if X is false? What happens to the narrative?
- The narrative uses the word Y. They could have said Z. Why Y and not Z? What does Y do that Z doesn't?
- The narrative presents A as obvious. In what contexts is A not obvious? Who would disagree, and why?

Interrogation Checkpoint (SOP-IOT Phase 2–3):
- Am I finding the real load-bearing assumptions, or just the visible ones?
- Which assumptions am I most tempted to leave unquestioned? (That's where my own bias lives)
- Have I tested whether these assumptions are universal or context-specific? (SOP-IOT Phase 4)
- What foundational assumption, if removed, would cause the entire narrative to collapse?

## Part Five: Evidentiary Audit (SOP-ER Phases 3–5)

### 5.1 Purpose

Test the narrative's factual claims against the evidentiary record. This is where SOP-ER's research methodology provides the backbone — source tracing, reliability tiering, and citation chains.

### 5.2 Process

**Step 1 — Claim Verification:**
For every claim logged in D.2 (Claim Register), conduct SOP-ER Phase 3 (Literature Hunt):
- What evidence exists that supports this claim?
- What evidence exists that contradicts this claim?
- What is the primary source of this claim? (Trace back — don't accept secondary citations)
- Has this claim been replicated, verified, or independently confirmed?

**Step 2 — Reliability Tiering:**
Apply SOP-ER Phase 4 (Critical Evaluation) to every source the narrative cites or implies:

| Tier | Description |
|---|---|
| **Tier 1** | Peer-reviewed, replicated, transparent methodology |
| **Tier 2** | Peer-reviewed, sound methodology, not yet replicated |
| **Tier 3** | Credible institution, not peer-reviewed |
| **Tier 4** | Informative but biased, limited methodology |
| **Tier 5** | Significant problems identified |

**Step 3 — The Data-Headline Test:**
Does the narrative's data actually support its headline claims? Read the actual data/methodology, not just the narrative's summary. Common failure modes:
- Correlation presented as causation
- Relative risk presented without absolute risk
- Cherry-picked timeframes that show a trend that disappears with longer data
- Anecdotal evidence generalized to populations
- Statistics cited without sample size, methodology, or confidence intervals

**Step 4 — The Discussion Test (from SOP-ER):**
Does the narrative honestly name its own limitations? A narrative that acknowledges what it doesn't know and can't prove is more trustworthy than one that claims certainty. Overconfidence is a red flag.

**Step 5 — Source Laundering Detection:**
Does the narrative cite sources that cite each other in a closed loop, creating an appearance of independent confirmation when the claims actually trace back to a single origin? This is authority accumulation through repetition — each citation adds apparent credibility, but no new evidence is introduced.

Update D.2 (Claim Register) with verification status for each claim: VERIFIED / PARTIALLY VERIFIED / UNVERIFIED / FALSIFIED / UNFALSIFIABLE. Update D.7 (Evidence Audit Trail) with full source provenance.

Interrogation Checkpoint (SOP-IOT Phase 3):
- Am I applying the same evidentiary standard to claims I agree with and claims I disagree with?
- Have I traced claims to primary sources, or am I accepting secondary citations?
- Am I detecting source laundering — circular citation that creates false authority?
- What would I need to see to accept a claim I currently doubt? What would I need to see to doubt a claim I currently accept?

## Part Six: Rhetorical Forensics

### 6.1 Purpose

Identify and catalog every rhetorical, persuasive, and manipulative technique deployed in the narrative. This is the narrative's paintbrush — the tools used to create the desired impression.

### 6.2 The Rhetorical Technique Taxonomy

**Framing Techniques:**
- **Selection framing** — What facts are included and excluded? The boundary of the frame determines what's visible.
- **Emphasis framing** — What gets foregrounded and what gets backgrounded? Equal facts can create opposite impressions through emphasis alone.
- **Definitional framing** — How are key terms defined? "Enhanced interrogation" vs. "torture" is the same act under different frames.
- **Temporal framing** — Where does the story start and end? Starting the clock at different points creates different narratives from the same events.
- **Binary framing** — Presenting complex issues as two-sided when they have many dimensions. "You're either with us or against us."

**Emotional Techniques:**
- **Fear appeals** — Creating urgency through threat (real or manufactured)
- **Anger appeals** — Directing outrage at a target
- **Hope appeals** — Promising a better future contingent on accepting the narrative
- **Disgust appeals** — Creating visceral negative responses to associate targets with contamination or moral failing
- **Belonging appeals** — "People like us believe X" — using social identity as leverage

**Authority Techniques:**
- **Credential citation** — Invoking expertise (sometimes relevant, sometimes not)
- **Bandwagon claims** — "Everyone agrees that..."
- **Testimonial** — Using a sympathetic figure to embody the narrative's claims
- **Institutional authority** — "Studies show..." without specifying which studies
- **Historical authority** — "This is how it's always been done"

**Narrative Construction Techniques:**
- **Character assignment** — Casting actors as heroes, villains, victims, or bystanders. Who gets which role and why?
- **Causal attribution** — What is presented as causing what? Causal claims are the narrative's engine.
- **Temporal ordering** — The sequence in which events are presented shapes perceived causation. Post hoc ergo propter hoc (after this, therefore because of this) is the most common narrative fallacy.
- **Resolution framing** — What is presented as the solution? Who benefits from that solution being adopted?
- **Moral framing** — What is presented as right/wrong? What moral framework is being invoked (and which ones are being excluded)?

**Language Techniques:**
- **Loaded language** — Words chosen for emotional impact rather than precision
- **Euphemism/dysphemism** — Softening or hardening the same reality through word choice
- **Glittering generalities** — Undefined virtue words ("freedom," "justice," "innovation") that sound positive but commit to nothing
- **Repetition** — Strategic repetition of key characterizations to build familiarity and acceptance
- **Passive voice concealment** — "Mistakes were made" (who made them?) vs. "We made mistakes"

### 6.3 Apply SOP-IM Methods 2–5

- **Method 2 (The Contradiction):** Where do the narrative's words say one thing but its structure, tone, or evidence suggest another?
- **Method 3 (The Reframe):** What happens if you reframe the narrative from a different perspective? Does the story still hold?
- **Method 4 (The Pattern):** What rhetorical patterns repeat across the narrative? Repetition reveals strategy.
- **Method 5 (The Counterfactual):** What words *could* have been used but weren't? What framing *could* have been chosen? The road not taken reveals the narrator's intent.

Log all identified techniques in D.4 (Rhetorical Technique Log) with specific examples from the text.

Interrogation Checkpoint (SOP-IOT Phase 4–5):
- Am I identifying techniques accurately, or over-interpreting normal rhetorical choices as manipulation?
- Not every rhetorical technique is manipulative — persuasion is inherent in communication. Am I distinguishing legitimate persuasion from manipulation?
- Am I noticing the techniques that align with my own views, or only the ones that oppose them?

## Part Seven: Omission Mapping

### 7.1 Purpose

Identify what the narrative does NOT say. Omissions are the negative space of narrative construction — often more revealing than what's included. A narrative gains its power not just from what it puts in frame, but from what it leaves out.

### 7.2 The Five Types of Omission

| Omission Type | Description | Detection Question |
|---|---|---|
| **Perspective Omission** | Voices, viewpoints, or stakeholders not represented | Who is affected by this story but never speaks in it? |
| **Evidence Omission** | Data, research, or facts that exist but aren't included | What relevant evidence was available but not cited? |
| **Context Omission** | Historical, cultural, or situational context left out | What context would change how this narrative is received? |
| **Alternative Omission** | Alternative explanations or framings not considered | What other explanations exist that the narrative doesn't mention? |
| **Consequence Omission** | Outcomes, costs, or downstream effects not addressed | What are the consequences of accepting this narrative that it doesn't discuss? |

### 7.3 Process

1. For each omission type, systematically ask the detection question
2. Cross-reference with the Schema (from Phase 1) — the landscape scoping often reveals what the narrative left out
3. Cross-reference with the Evidentiary Audit (Phase 5) — contradicting evidence that exists but wasn't included is the most damning type of omission
4. Assess whether each omission is likely **inadvertent** (the narrator didn't know), **structural** (the medium or format prevented inclusion), or **strategic** (the narrator chose to exclude it)

Log all omissions in D.5 (Omission Register) with type, evidence of the omission, and assessment of likely cause (inadvertent/structural/strategic).

### 7.4 The Silence Test

Ask: If this narrative included everything it currently omits, would it still reach the same conclusion? If the answer is no, the omissions are load-bearing — the narrative depends on what it leaves out.

Interrogation Checkpoint (SOP-IOT Phase 5):
- Am I identifying genuine omissions, or demanding the narrative address everything? (No narrative can include everything — the question is whether *material* information was omitted)
- Am I distinguishing between inadvertent, structural, and strategic omissions?
- What would change if the largest omission were restored?

## Part Eight: Interest Analysis

### 8.1 Purpose

Map whose interests are served by this narrative being accepted as presented. Follow the incentives. Narratives don't emerge from nowhere — they serve purposes, and those purposes have beneficiaries.

### 8.2 Process

**Step 1 — Identify Beneficiaries:**
- Who gains power, money, status, authority, or legitimacy if this narrative is accepted?
- Who avoids accountability, scrutiny, or consequences if this narrative is accepted?
- Who is harmed, marginalized, or delegitimized if this narrative is accepted?

**Step 2 — Map the Incentive Structure:**
- What is the narrator's relationship to the beneficiaries?
- Is the narrator a beneficiary? An agent of a beneficiary? An unwitting amplifier?
- What economic, political, social, or institutional incentives shape the narrative's construction?

**Step 3 — Test for Alignment:**
- Does the narrative consistently favor one set of interests over others?
- Do the omissions (Phase 7) systematically protect one party?
- Do the rhetorical techniques (Phase 6) consistently direct emotion toward or away from specific actors?

**Step 4 — Apply the Cui Bono Framework:**
For each major narrative choice (framing, omission, emphasis, characterization), ask: Who benefits from this specific choice? If the answers consistently point to the same beneficiary, the narrative is likely serving that interest — whether by design or by captured worldview.

### 8.3 Interest ≠ Conspiracy

Important boundary: Interest analysis identifies structural incentives, not secret plots. A pharmaceutical company funding a study that happens to support its product is an interest alignment — not necessarily fraud. A news outlet's ownership influencing its editorial slant is an interest structure — not necessarily a conspiracy. The goal is to surface these structures so they can be weighed, not to declare every narrative a deliberate deception.

Log all findings in D.6 (Interest Map) with specific evidence for each interest alignment identified.

Interrogation Checkpoint (SOP-IOT Phase 5–6):
- Am I identifying real interest alignments supported by evidence, or constructing a conspiracy from coincidence?
- Am I applying interest analysis to narratives I agree with as rigorously as narratives I disagree with?
- Would the narrative look different if the narrator had different incentives? How?

## Part Nine: Counter-Narrative Construction

### 9.1 Purpose

Construct at least one alternative narrative from the same underlying facts — but with different framing, different emphasis, different inclusion/exclusion choices, and different assumptions. This is the ultimate test of the original narrative's construction: if the same facts can tell a fundamentally different story, the original narrative's presentation is a choice, not a necessity.

### 9.2 Process

**Step 1 — Reassemble from Facts:**
Using only verified claims from D.2 (Claim Register), build a new narrative that:
- Starts at a different point in the timeline
- Centers a different actor or stakeholder
- Uses a different causal framework
- Includes what the original narrative omitted
- Omits what the original narrative emphasized

**Step 2 — Compare Narratives Side by Side:**

| Dimension | Original Narrative | Counter-Narrative |
|---|---|---|
| Central actor | Who is centered? | Who is centered instead? |
| Starting point | Where does the clock start? | Where does it start instead? |
| Causal claim | What is presented as the cause? | What alternative cause is possible? |
| Moral frame | Who is the hero/villain? | How do those roles shift? |
| Resolution | What solution is implied? | What alternative solution emerges? |
| Key omission | What was left out? | What happens when it's included? |

**Step 3 — Assess Plausibility:**
Apply SOP-IOT Phase 5 (Generate Alternative Hypotheses):
- Which narrative is best supported by verified evidence?
- Which requires fewer assumptions?
- Which accounts for more of the known facts?
- Could both be partially true?

This is NOT about proving the counter-narrative is correct. It is about demonstrating that the original narrative is a construction — one of multiple possible stories that could be told from the same evidence base.

Log counter-narratives in D.8 (Counter-Narrative Log) with evidence basis and plausibility assessment.

Interrogation Checkpoint (SOP-IOT Phase 5):
- Is my counter-narrative genuinely evidence-based, or am I constructing a strawman?
- Am I building a counter-narrative to test the original, or because I prefer the alternative?
- Could both narratives be partially true simultaneously?

## Part Ten: Analyst Self-Interrogation (SOP-SI)

### 10.1 Purpose

Surface the deconstructor's own biases, projections, and emotional reactions. The analyst is not a neutral instrument — they bring their own assumptions, worldview, and emotional responses to the deconstruction. This phase prevents the deconstruction itself from becoming a new, unexamined narrative.

### 10.2 Process

**Apply SOP-SI Method 1 (Three Voices Applied to Self):**

1. **Transcription:** What did I actually do during this deconstruction? Which phases did I spend the most time on? Which did I rush through?
2. **Observation:** How did I react to this narrative? Where did I feel anger, validation, disgust, excitement, boredom? What were my body's signals?
3. **Interpretation:** What did I make it mean? Did I approach this narrative as something to understand, or something to destroy? What was my emotional investment in the outcome?

**Apply SOP-SI Method 5 (The Reframe Applied to Self):**
- My automatic interpretation of this narrative was ___. Is that interpretation true?
- What evidence supports my reading? What evidence contradicts it?
- If someone I trust told me this narrative was accurate, would I reconsider? Why or why not?

**Apply SOP-SI Method 6 (The Timeline):**
- When did I first form my opinion about this narrative? Before or after the deconstruction?
- Has the deconstruction changed my view? If not, why not? Am I genuinely open to having my view changed?

### 10.3 The Mirror Rule

If the deconstructor cannot identify at least three ways their own biases, worldview, or emotional reactions may have influenced the analysis, the deconstruction is incomplete. Self-interrogation is not optional — it is a structural requirement.

Log all findings in D.9 (Analyst Bias Log).

Interrogation Checkpoint (SOP-SI, SOP-IOT Phase 6):
- Am I deconstructing this narrative because it deserves scrutiny, or because I disagree with its conclusion?
- What would I find if I applied this same deconstruction to a narrative I agree with?
- Where am I most confident in my analysis? (That's often where I'm least critical)
- What would the narrator say about my deconstruction? Would any of their objections be valid?

## Part Eleven: Synthesis and Documentation

### 11.1 Purpose

Produce the final deconstruction document — a structured, evidence-based analysis that lays bare the narrative's architecture while maintaining epistemic humility about what remains unknown.

### 11.2 The Deconstruction Brief Structure

1. **Narrative Summary** — What the narrative says (Surface Capture, Phase 2)
2. **Layer Analysis** — How the narrative constructs its meaning (Layer Separation, Phase 3)
3. **Assumption Architecture** — What the narrative requires you to believe (Assumption Excavation, Phase 4)
4. **Evidentiary Assessment** — What holds up and what doesn't (Evidentiary Audit, Phase 5)
5. **Rhetorical Technique Catalog** — How the narrative persuades (Rhetorical Forensics, Phase 6)
6. **Omission Analysis** — What the narrative leaves out (Omission Mapping, Phase 7)
7. **Interest Assessment** — Who benefits (Interest Analysis, Phase 8)
8. **Counter-Narrative(s)** — Alternative stories from the same facts (Counter-Narrative Construction, Phase 9)
9. **Analyst Limitations** — How the deconstructor's own biases may have influenced the analysis (Analyst Self-Interrogation, Phase 10)
10. **Confidence and Unknowns** — What is known, unknown, and unknowable (SOP-IOT Phase 7)

### 11.3 The Epistemic Humility Requirement

Every Deconstruction Brief must include an explicit statement of:
- What the deconstruction established with high confidence
- What remains uncertain
- What the deconstruction could not determine (and why)
- What conditions would warrant revisiting the analysis
- What the strongest defense of the original narrative would be

### 11.4 Citation Chain Verification

Before finalizing, verify the Three-Link Citation Chain for every factual claim in the Brief:
- Claim in Brief → D.2 (Claim Register) with verification status → D.7 (Evidence Audit Trail) with full source provenance
- Any claim that cannot complete this chain gets flagged or removed

### 11.5 Apply SOP-IOT Phase 7 (Provisional Synthesis and Documentation)

Classify all findings:
- **Known** — Well-supported by evidence from multiple sources
- **Unknown** — Insufficient evidence but potentially knowable with further investigation
- **Unknowable** — Inherently beyond current ability to determine (e.g., narrator's true internal motivation)

## Part Twelve: Standalone Use and Adaptation

### 12.1 SOP-DN Beyond TLV Takedowns

This SOP is designed as a general-purpose narrative deconstruction methodology. While it integrates fully with the TLV Takedown Workflow (providing the analytical engine for Phase 4: Meaning Extraction and Phase 6: Translation Test), it works independently for:

- **Media analysis** — Deconstructing news coverage, documentaries, opinion pieces
- **Political analysis** — Deconstructing speeches, campaign messaging, policy framings
- **Institutional analysis** — Deconstructing corporate communications, internal narratives, organizational stories
- **Legal analysis** — Deconstructing arguments, witness narratives, institutional positions
- **Interpersonal use** — Deconstructing personal narratives others present to you (with ethical boundaries — see Part 12.3)
- **Self-application** — Deconstructing your own presenting narratives (use with SOP-SI)
- **Academic research** — Discourse analysis, framing analysis, narrative analysis
- **Marketing/advertising analysis** — Deconstructing brand narratives, value propositions, positioning strategies

### 12.2 Scaling Time Investment

| Deconstruction Type | Phases Used | Time Investment |
|---|---|---|
| Quick narrative check (social media post, headline) | Phases 2–4 (abbreviated) | 10–30 minutes |
| Article/speech deconstruction | Phases 1–8 | 2–6 hours |
| Documentary/series deconstruction | All phases, multiple iterations | 10–40 hours |
| Institutional narrative deconstruction (TLV Takedown grade) | All phases, full depth, team-based | 40–100+ hours |

### 12.3 Ethical Boundaries

Narrative deconstruction is a power tool. Like all TLV methods, it comes with ethical obligations:

- **Deconstruction is not a weapon against individuals in personal relationships** — deconstructing a partner's narrative in an argument is interrogation without consent (SOP-IM ethical boundary)
- **Deconstruction does not assume bad faith** — surface structure, then assess intent based on evidence
- **Deconstruction must be applied reflexively** — if you only deconstruct narratives you disagree with, you're not doing deconstruction; you're doing confirmation bias with better tools
- **Deconstruction results must be presented with epistemic humility** — you found the architecture of the narrative; you didn't necessarily find "the truth"

### 12.4 Integration with Other TLV SOPs

| SOP | How SOP-DN Calls It | When |
|---|---|---|
| **SOP-SP** | Schema Priming initializes the deconstruction landscape | Phase 1 |
| **SOP-ER** | Research methodology verifies/falsifies claims | Phase 5 |
| **SOP-IOT** | Interrogation Checkpoints at every phase boundary | All phases |
| **SOP-IM** | Micro-methods deployed as analytical tools | Phases 3, 4, 6, 7, 8 |

## Part Thirteen: Example Deconstructions

### Example 1: Quick Deconstruction — Social Media Claim (20 Minutes)

**Narrative:** A viral social media post claims: "Country X banned all pesticides and crop yields increased 40% in three years."

**Phase 1 (Schema — 3 min):** Quick scoping — search for the claim, Country X agricultural policy, competing accounts.

**Phase 2 (Surface Capture — 1 min):** Post captured verbatim. Claim logged: "Country X banned all pesticides" (UNVERIFIED). Claim logged: "crop yields increased 40%" (UNVERIFIED).

**Phase 3 (Layer Separation — 2 min):**
- Semantic: Two factual claims linked causally
- Pragmatic: Presented as simple cause-and-effect; viral format implies "why can't we do this?"
- Psychological: Engineers frustration with status quo and hope for simple solution

**Phase 4 (Assumption Excavation — 3 min):**
- Assumes a total ban is even possible for a major agricultural country
- Assumes the 40% increase was caused by the ban (causal assumption)
- Assumes no other factors (weather, new varieties, irrigation, subsidy changes) contributed
- Assumes the data is real

**Phase 5 (Evidentiary Audit — 8 min):**
- Claim 1: Country X did not ban ALL pesticides — it banned a specific category. PARTIALLY VERIFIED.
- Claim 2: Yields increased approximately 15% for some crops, decreased for others. Net effect disputed. FALSIFIED as stated.
- Data source: The statistic traces to an advocacy organization's press release, not government data. Tier 4.

**Phase 6–7 (Rhetorical + Omission — 3 min):**
- Technique: Glittering generality ("all pesticides"), causal oversimplification
- Key omission: The crops where yields decreased; the government subsidies that cushioned the transition; the increase in organic certification costs borne by small farmers

**Result:** The core narrative — "ban pesticides, yields go up" — is a dramatic oversimplification of a complex, mixed-outcome policy. The presenting narrative selects the positive results, omits the negative, and presents correlation as causation.

### Example 2: Full Deconstruction — Institutional Communication (Multi-Day)

**Narrative:** A corporation's public statement following an environmental incident: "We have always been committed to environmental stewardship. The incident was caused by an unprecedented weather event. We have already taken steps to prevent recurrence and are working closely with regulators."

**Phase 1 (Schema — 2 hours):** Scope the landscape: company's environmental record, regulatory filings, previous incidents, independent reporting, community accounts, scientific assessment of the weather claim.

**Phase 2 (Surface Capture):** Statement captured verbatim. Four claims extracted:
1. "Always committed to environmental stewardship" — UNVERIFIED
2. "Caused by unprecedented weather event" — UNVERIFIED
3. "Already taken steps to prevent recurrence" — UNVERIFIED
4. "Working closely with regulators" — UNVERIFIED

**Phase 3 (Layer Separation):**
- Semantic: Claims of commitment, external causation, remedial action, cooperation
- Pragmatic: Corporate crisis communication template — open with values, externalize cause, demonstrate action, invoke authority relationship. Tone: measured, professional, deflecting.
- Psychological: Engineers reassurance, minimizes perceived threat, positions company as responsible actor and victim of nature simultaneously

**Phase 4 (Assumption Excavation):**
- "Always" requires no significant environmental violations in company history (testable)
- "Unprecedented weather event" assumes the weather was truly outside design parameters (testable)
- "Unprecedented" implies the event could not have been anticipated (challengeable — climate models may have predicted increased frequency)
- "Already taken steps" assumes the steps are adequate (untested)
- "Working closely with regulators" assumes the relationship is cooperative rather than adversarial (verifiable)

**Phase 5 (Evidentiary Audit):**
- Claim 1: Company has three previous EPA violations in the past decade. FALSIFIED as stated.
- Claim 2: Weather event was within the range predicted by 2019 climate risk assessment commissioned by the company. FALSIFIED — the company's own consultants predicted this type of event.
- Claim 3: "Steps taken" amount to a press release commitment, no engineering changes documented yet. UNVERIFIED.
- Claim 4: Regulator's public statement describes the relationship as "ongoing investigation," not "close collaboration." CONTRADICTED by independent source.

**Phase 6 (Rhetorical Forensics):**
- "Always committed" — glittering generality, unfalsifiable as stated
- "Unprecedented" — definitional framing (redefines a predicted event as unpredictable)
- Passive voice: "The incident was caused by" — agency removed from company
- "Working closely" — euphemism for "being investigated by"

**Phase 7 (Omission Mapping):**
- Perspective: No community voices. No affected workers. No independent environmental assessment.
- Evidence: Company's own climate risk assessment (which predicted the event type) not mentioned
- Context: Previous violations not mentioned
- Consequence: Health impacts on surrounding community not addressed

**Phase 8 (Interest Analysis):**
- Beneficiary: Company avoids regulatory consequences and liability exposure
- Narrator incentive: Legal team likely drafted; minimizing culpability is the structural incentive
- Pattern: Omissions systematically protect the company; every rhetorical choice deflects responsibility

**Phase 9 (Counter-Narrative):**
Same facts, different framing: "A company with three prior environmental violations experienced an incident of the type its own climate consultants predicted in 2019. The company's infrastructure was not upgraded to meet the predicted conditions. The surrounding community reports ongoing health concerns. Regulators have opened an investigation."

**Phase 10 (Self-Interrogation):**
Analyst bias check: Am I predisposed to distrust corporate communications? (Probably.) Did that predisposition affect my evidentiary standard? (Possibly — but the evidence independently supports the deconstruction.) Would I apply this same rigor to a community organization's communication? (I should, and the methodology requires it.)

### Example 3: Collaborative Team Deconstruction (Using SOP-CI)

**Narrative:** A policy proposal framed as a "reform" with bipartisan support.

**Phase 1:** Team of four analysts scopes independently, then synthesizes using SOP-CI Method 9 (1-2-4-All): 1 minute silent reflection → 2 minutes paired sharing → 4 minutes quartet synthesis → full team agreement on scope and initial hypotheses.

**Phases 2–5:** Divided among team members by specialization (one handles evidentiary audit, one handles rhetorical forensics, etc.), with the Deconstruction Ledger as shared infrastructure.

**Phase 8 (Interest Analysis):** SOP-CI Method 10 (Pre-Mortem) applied: "It's three months after this policy passes. It failed to deliver its promised benefits. Why?" Team brainstorms failure scenarios → reveals assumptions in the reform narrative that the policy proposal never defended.

**Phase 9 (Counter-Narrative):** SOP-CI Method 2 (Multiple Perspectives) — each analyst constructs a counter-narrative from a different stakeholder perspective. Compare: what does the policy look like from each angle?

**Phase 11 (Synthesis):** SOP-CI Method 1 (Collective Descent) — What did our deconstruction find? (facts) → How does the narrative construct its persuasiveness? (mechanism) → Why was this narrative constructed this way? (interest analysis)

## Appendix D: Interrogation Checkpoint Summary

**After Phase 1 (Schema):** Am I approaching with curiosity or predetermined conclusion?

**After Phase 2 (Surface Capture):** Am I capturing or already interpreting?

**After Phase 3 (Layer Separation):** Am I keeping Layers 1–3 genuinely separate?

**After Phase 4 (Assumptions):** Which assumptions am I most tempted to leave unquestioned?

**After Phase 5 (Evidence):** Am I applying the same standard to claims I agree with and disagree with?

**After Phase 6 (Rhetoric):** Am I distinguishing persuasion from manipulation?

**After Phase 7 (Omissions):** Am I identifying material omissions, or demanding the narrative address everything?

**After Phase 8 (Interests):** Am I identifying structural incentives or constructing conspiracies?

**After Phase 9 (Counter-Narrative):** Am I building an evidence-based alternative or a preferred alternative?

**After Phase 10 (Self-Interrogation):** Can I identify at least three ways my biases may have influenced this analysis?

**After Phase 11 (Synthesis):** Does the Brief honestly state what remains unknown and unknowable?

## Appendix E: The Three Laws of Narrative Deconstruction

**First Law — Every Narrative is a Construction.**
No narrative is "just the facts." Every narrative selects, sequences, frames, emphasizes, and omits. Recognizing this is not cynicism — it is structural awareness.

**Second Law — Deconstruction is Reflexive.**
Any deconstruction that does not interrogate the deconstructor is incomplete. The analyst's biases are as much a part of the analysis as the narrative's assumptions.

**Third Law — Deconstruction is Not Destruction.**
The goal is not to prove the narrative wrong. The goal is to make its architecture visible — so that the audience can make informed choices about whether to accept, modify, or reject it. Some narratives survive deconstruction with increased credibility. That's a valid outcome.

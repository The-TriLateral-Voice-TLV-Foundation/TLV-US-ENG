## Executive Summary

The **Methods for Collaborative Interrogation of Thought** is a Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) for systematically examining shared thinking, surfacing hidden group assumptions, resolving conflicts, and building shared understanding through structured multi-person inquiry.

**Core Principle:** Groups compound projection. When individuals operate on unexamined assumptions, the damage is personal. When groups do it, the damage is organizational, institutional, and civilizational. Collaborative interrogation provides systematic tools for groups to examine their collective thinking before acting on it.

**Why this matters:**

Groups constantly operate on unexamined shared assumptions. These assumptions:
- Amplify individual blind spots through conformity pressure
- Create shared narratives that resist correction
- Allow power dynamics to masquerade as consensus
- Produce decisions that no individual would make alone
- Generate predictable failure patterns at organizational scale

By interrogating thought collaboratively, groups:
- Surface assumptions that no single member can see alone
- Triangulate understanding through multiple perspectives
- Resolve conflicts at the level of underlying needs, not surface positions
- Build shared meaning that survives implementation
- Make better collective decisions

---

## Mission

To provide facilitators, teams, and organizations with systematic methods for examining shared thinking, surfacing collective projection and assumption layers, resolving interpersonal and group conflicts, and arriving at more rigorous shared understanding before making decisions or judgments.

---

## Core Understanding

Individual interrogation addresses a single mind's projections. But groups introduce a harder problem: **shared projections that feel like truth because everyone agrees.**

This is the "white marble" problem at organizational scale. When one scholar projects meaning onto unpainted marble, it's an individual error. When an entire discipline does it for centuries—building institutions, curricula, and cultural identity on the projection—it becomes civilizational. The error persists not because no one questioned it, but because group dynamics (conformity, authority deference, shared investment in the narrative) suppressed questioning.

Every organization, team, and community carries "white marble" assumptions: shared beliefs that feel obvious, that "everyone knows," that form the unquestioned foundation of how the group operates. Collaborative interrogation exists to surface those assumptions before they calcify into doctrine.

This SOP provides the methods. It builds on the [core Interrogation of Thought SOP](./Methods-for-the-Interrogation-of-Thought-SOP-RC0001.1-US-English.md), which defines the Seven Phases and philosophical foundations. This document extends those principles to the unique dynamics of multi-person contexts—where power, safety, airtime, and competing perspectives create both greater risk and greater opportunity for deeper understanding.

## Part One: Why Collaborative Interrogation is Different

### 1.1 Individual vs. Collaborative Interrogation

| Aspect | Individual | Collaborative |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| **Power dynamics** | 1-to-1 or self | Multiple, complex |
| **Perspective** | Single | Multiple (can triangulate) |
| **Psychological safety** | Depends on trust in facilitator | Depends on group norms |
| **Time** | Flexible | Must respect group time |
| **Conflict** | Lower risk | Higher risk (disagreement surfaces) |
| **Outcome** | Single understanding | Shared or explicitly divergent understanding |

### 1.2 Unique Challenges

1. **Airtime imbalance:** Some voices dominate, others are silenced
2. **Group pressure:** Conformity bias suppresses honest inquiry
3. **Conflict avoidance:** Groups often avoid difficult questions to maintain harmony
4. **Competing agendas:** Participants may have different goals for the session
5. **Unequal power:** Hierarchies (boss/employee, expert/novice) distort dialogue

### 1.3 Unique Opportunities

1. **Triangulation:** Multiple perspectives surface blind spots
2. **Collective wisdom:** Group knows more than any individual
3. **Real-time correction:** Misinterpretations can be challenged immediately
4. **Shared meaning-making:** Group builds common understanding together
5. **Accountability:** Public commitment to insights strengthens follow-through

## Part Two: Roles in Collaborative Interrogation

### 2.1 Facilitator

**Responsibilities:**
- Hold the interrogation process (not the content)
- Ensure psychological safety
- Manage airtime and power dynamics
- Ask clarifying questions
- Prevent destructive conflict
- Track time and progress

**Skills needed:**
- TLV interrogation training
- Conflict mediation experience
- Cultural competence
- Emotional regulation (stay neutral under tension)

**What facilitator does NOT do:**
- Provide answers or interpretations (unless explicitly the expert)
- Take sides in conflict
- Control what gets discussed (participants set content)

### 2.2 Participants

**Responsibilities:**
- Engage honestly with questions
- Listen actively to others
- Challenge ideas (not people)
- Mark own uncertainty
- Respect others' experience as valid

**Skills needed:**
- Basic TLV awareness (three voices, emotional vocabulary)
- Willingness to be vulnerable
- Tolerance for ambiguity

### 2.3 Observer/Scribe (Optional)

**Responsibilities:**
- Document what's said (TLV-style if appropriate)
- Note patterns, themes, tensions
- Provide summary at end
- Do NOT participate in discussion (to maintain neutrality)

**Skills needed:**
- TLV transcription/observation training
- Ability to remain neutral

### 2.4 Role Boundaries

**Clear separation required:**
- Facilitator does not participate as stakeholder
- Participants do not facilitate
- Observer does not contribute content

**Why:** Mixing roles creates confusion and power imbalances.

**Interrogation Checkpoint:**
- Are roles clearly defined and agreed upon before starting?
- Does the facilitator have any stake in the outcome? If so, bring in a neutral co-facilitator.
- Is the observer truly neutral, or do they have allegiances that could bias documentation?

## Part Three: Ground Rules and Safety

### 3.1 Minimum Ground Rules for All Sessions

Before starting collaborative interrogation, establish:

1. **Confidentiality** (if appropriate)
   - "What's said here stays here, unless we agree otherwise"
   - Define exceptions (mandatory reporting, public record, etc.)

2. **One voice at a time**
   - No interrupting
   - Facilitator manages turn-taking

3. **"I" statements, not "you" accusations**
   - "I felt hurt when..." instead of "You hurt me"
   - Describe experience, not intent

4. **Challenge ideas, not people**
   - "I disagree with that analysis" check
   - "You're wrong" cross

5. **Right to pass**
   - Anyone can decline to answer a question
   - No pressure to disclose beyond comfort level

6. **Assume good intent (until proven otherwise)**
   - Start from charitable interpretation
   - Name harm when it occurs, but don't assume malice

7. **Named time boundaries**
   - Session starts/ends at agreed time
   - Breaks scheduled
   - Participants can leave if needed (emergencies)

### 3.2 Psychological Safety Assessment

Before deep interrogation, facilitator should assess:

- **Trust level:** Do participants trust each other and facilitator?
- **Power dynamics:** Are there hierarchies that will inhibit honesty?
- **Stakes:** What's at risk if participants are vulnerable?
- **Capacity:** Are participants emotionally regulated enough to engage?

**If safety is low, adjust:**
- Keep questions less personal
- Focus on observable facts before interpretations
- Build trust through smaller exercises first
- Consider individual interviews instead of group

**Interrogation Checkpoint:**
- Has psychological safety been explicitly assessed, not just assumed?
- Are there participants whose silence signals discomfort rather than agreement?
- What power dynamics are present that have not been named?

## Part Four: Collaborative Methods

### Method 1: Collective Descent (Group Version of The Descent)

**Purpose:** Move group from surface understanding to deeper shared insight

**Process:**

1. **What (Semantic Layer):**
   - Facilitator: "What happened?" or "What was said?"
   - Each participant shares their version
   - Facilitator notes where accounts agree/diverge
   - Group identifies: "Here's what we all observed"

2. **How (Pragmatic Layer):**
   - Facilitator: "How did it happen?" or "How was it said?"
   - Participants describe tone, energy, dynamics
   - Use TLV Observation Voice principles (specific, falsifiable)
   - Group identifies: "Here's what we all noticed about how it unfolded"

3. **Why (Psychological Layer):**
   - Facilitator: "Why did it happen that way?"
   - Participants offer interpretations
   - Mark as interpretations, not facts
   - Group explores: "Here are the possible explanations"

**Example (Team Debrief):**

**Facilitator:** "Let's talk about yesterday's client meeting. What happened?"

**Participants share facts:**
- "Client said they were unhappy with timeline"
- "We presented three options"
- "They chose option B"

**Facilitator:** "How did it happen? What was the energy?"

**Participants share observations:**
- "Client's tone was sharp when discussing timeline"
- "Our team lead got defensive"
- "Room felt tense after that exchange"

**Facilitator:** "Why do you think it unfolded that way?"

**Participants offer interpretations:**
- "Client felt we weren't taking their concerns seriously"
- "Our lead felt attacked and went into protection mode"
- "We didn't acknowledge their frustration before presenting solutions"

**Interrogation Checkpoint:**
- Did all participants contribute at each layer, or did some voices dominate?
- Were interpretations clearly marked as interpretations, not presented as facts?
- Did the group resist the urge to jump to "why" before thoroughly exploring "what" and "how"?

---

### Method 2: Multiple Perspectives (Triangulation)

**Purpose:** Use multiple viewpoints to build fuller picture

**Process:**

1. **Individual perspectives first:**
   - Each participant shares their interpretation independently (written or verbal)
   - No discussion yet

2. **Facilitator maps perspectives:**
   - "Here's what Person A sees"
   - "Here's what Person B sees"
   - "Here's where they overlap"
   - "Here's where they diverge"

3. **Group explores divergences:**
   - Not to prove who's "right," but to understand why different perspectives exist
   - "What is Person A seeing that Person B isn't?"
   - "What context does Person B have that Person A lacks?"

4. **Synthesis:**
   - Group decides: Shared understanding? Or legitimate divergent views?
   - Document both if unresolvable

**Example (Conflict Mediation):**

**Party A:** "You never listen to my ideas in meetings."

**Party B:** "I do listen! You just don't speak up."

**Facilitator:** "Let me map what I'm hearing. Person A experiences their ideas as unheard. Person B experiences themselves as open to hearing. Both can be true. Let's explore: What's happening in meetings that creates these different experiences?"

**Result:** Discovery that Party A offers ideas informally before meetings, Party B only registers ideas raised formally in meeting, neither knew the other's process.

**Interrogation Checkpoint:**
- Were individual perspectives captured *before* group discussion could create conformity?
- Did the facilitator present divergences neutrally, without signaling which perspective seems "more correct"?
- Is the synthesis genuinely shared, or did the loudest voice win?

### Method 3: The Fishbowl (Observed Dialogue)

**Purpose:** Create space for smaller group to dialogue deeply while others observe

**Process:**

1. **Inner circle (3-5 people):**
   - Engage in interrogation dialogue
   - Use any individual method (Descent, Reframe, etc.)
   - Forget the observers; focus on each other

2. **Outer circle (everyone else):**
   - Observe silently
   - Note patterns, themes, tensions
   - Hold questions for later

3. **Debrief with full group:**
   - What did observers notice?
   - What did inner circle experience?
   - What insights emerged?

**When to use:**
- Large groups where not everyone can participate actively
- When specific stakeholders need to work through something while others learn
- To model interrogation for others

**Interrogation Checkpoint:**
- Did the inner circle engage authentically, or perform for observers?
- Did observers notice patterns the inner circle couldn't see from inside?
- Were power dynamics considered when selecting inner circle participants?

---

### Method 4: Rotating Facilitator (Peer Interrogation)

**Purpose:** Distribute facilitation power among participants

**Process:**

1. **Round 1:** Person A facilitates interrogation of Person B
2. **Round 2:** Person B facilitates interrogation of Person C
3. **Continue:** Until all participants have been both facilitator and interrogated
4. **Debrief:** What patterns emerged across all rounds?

**When to use:**
- Peer teams (no formal hierarchy)
- Building facilitation skills
- Avoiding over-dependence on single facilitator

**Interrogation Checkpoint:**
- Did each facilitator maintain neutrality, or did personal dynamics leak into the questioning?
- Were all participants equally rigorous, or did some rounds go deeper than others?
- What does the pattern across rounds reveal about the group's shared assumptions?

## Part Five: Conflict-Specific Methods

### Method 5: The Looping (Active Listening Protocol)

**Purpose:** Ensure each person feels heard before moving to solutions

**Process:**

1. **Person A speaks:**
   - Shares their experience/perspective (2-3 minutes)

2. **Person B loops back:**
   - "What I'm hearing you say is [paraphrase]. Did I get that right?"
   - Person A corrects or confirms

3. **Repeat until Person A feels heard:**
   - "Yes, that's exactly what I mean"

4. **Switch:**
   - Person B speaks, Person A loops

5. **Only after both feel heard:**
   - Move to problem-solving

**Why this works:**
- Most conflict escalates because people don't feel heard
- Looping forces active listening
- Prevents premature solutions before understanding problem

**Interrogation Checkpoint:**
- Did each person genuinely feel heard, or just agree to move on?
- Was the loop-back accurate, or did the listener distort through their own assumptions?
- Did the facilitator intervene when loops became mechanical rather than genuine?

### Method 6: The Ladder of Inference (Surfacing Assumptions)

**Purpose:** Identify where interpretations diverged from observed facts

**Process:**

1. **Observable data:**
   - "What actually happened?" (facts only)

2. **Selected data:**
   - "What did you notice?" (everyone selects different details)

3. **Interpretation:**
   - "What did you think it meant?"

4. **Assumptions:**
   - "What did you assume was true?"

5. **Conclusions:**
   - "What did you conclude?"

6. **Actions:**
   - "What did you do as a result?"

**Map both parties' ladders side by side:**
- Often same facts, different selections, different interpretations

**Example:**

**Observable data:** "Person A said 'I don't have time for this' and left meeting"

**Person B's ladder:**
- Selected: "Dismissive tone, didn't wait for response"
- Interpreted: "Person A doesn't care about this project"
- Assumed: "Person A is not a team player"
- Concluded: "I can't rely on Person A"
- Action: "Stopped including Person A in planning"

**Person A's ladder:**
- Selected: "I had another meeting in 5 minutes, needed to leave"
- Interpreted: "I communicated urgency"
- Assumed: "Team would understand I'm overbooked"
- Concluded: "They'll catch me up later"
- Action: "Attended other meeting"

**Resolution:** Person B didn't know about time constraint; Person A didn't know tone sounded dismissive. Miscommunication, not malice.

**Interrogation Checkpoint:**
- At which rung of the ladder did the interpretations first diverge?
- What assumptions would need to be true for each person's ladder to make sense?
- Are there rungs either person is unwilling to examine? That's where the real work is.

---

### Method 7: The Underlying Interests (Positions vs. Needs)

**Purpose:** Move from competing positions to underlying needs

**Process:**

1. **Identify positions:**
   - "What do you want?" (surface demands)

2. **Surface interests:**
   - "Why do you want that?" (underlying needs)

3. **Explore:**
   - "What would meeting that need give you?"
   - Keep asking "why" until you reach core needs

4. **Look for shared interests:**
   - Often, underlying needs are compatible even when positions conflict

**Example:**

**Position A:** "I want to work from home full-time"  
**Position B:** "I want everyone in office"

**Surface conflict:** Incompatible positions

**Interrogate interests:**
- Person A: Why work from home? Need: Focus time without interruptions, flexibility for childcare
- Person B: Why office? Need: Team cohesion, spontaneous collaboration, manage as team

**Shared interest:** Both want productive team with strong relationships

**Creative solution:** Hybrid schedule with protected focus time and intentional collaboration time

**Interrogation Checkpoint:**
- Did both parties reach genuine core needs, or stop at rationalized positions?
- Are the "shared interests" truly shared, or is the facilitator projecting alignment?
- Does the creative solution actually honor both parties' needs, or does one side compromise more?

## Part Six: Team Learning Methods

### Method 8: After-Action Review (Military-Derived)

**Purpose:** Extract lessons from completed project/event

**Structure (4 Questions):**

1. **What was supposed to happen?**
   - Original plan, expectations, goals

2. **What actually happened?**
   - Facts, observations (TLV Transcription + Observation)

3. **Why was there a difference?**
   - Interpretations, root causes (TLV Interpretation)

4. **What will we do differently next time?**
   - Actionable lessons

**Key principle:** Blame-free zone. Focus on learning, not punishment.

**Interrogation Checkpoint:**
- Is the "blame-free zone" genuinely honored, or does blame leak through tone and framing?
- Are "lessons learned" specific and actionable, or vague platitudes?
- Who owns each lesson? Without ownership, lessons evaporate.

---

### Method 9: Liberating Structures: 1-2-4-All

**Purpose:** Ensure all voices heard before group synthesis

**Process:**

1. **Pose question to group** (e.g., "Why did this project succeed?")

2. **1 minute: Individual silent reflection**
   - Each person writes their answer

3. **2 minutes: Pair sharing**
   - Share with one partner, notice patterns

4. **4 minutes: Quartet sharing**
   - Two pairs join, synthesize insights

5. **All: Group shares**
   - Each quartet shares key insights to full group
   - Facilitator captures themes

**Why this works:**
- Introverts get thinking time before speaking
- No single voice dominates
- Builds from individual to collective gradually

**Interrogation Checkpoint:**
- Did the "All" phase actually reflect the diversity of individual views, or did synthesis flatten them?
- Were quieter participants' ideas preserved through the escalation, or lost at each stage?
- What themes appeared in individuals that disappeared by the group stage? That's worth investigating.

---

### Method 10: Pre-Mortem (Prospective Interrogation)

**Purpose:** Surface risks and assumptions before starting project

**Process:**

1. **Imagine the future:**
   - "It's 6 months from now. This project has failed catastrophically. Why?"

2. **Individual brainstorm:**
   - Each person writes failure scenarios (5 minutes)

3. **Share scenarios:**
   - Go around, each shares one failure scenario

4. **Group identifies patterns:**
   - What failure modes appeared multiple times?
   - What assumptions are we making that could be wrong?

5. **Mitigate in advance:**
   - For top 3-5 risks, plan how to prevent or mitigate

**Why this works:**
- Permission to voice concerns without seeming "negative"
- Surfaces unspoken worries
- Proactive risk management

**Interrogation Checkpoint:**
- Were the failure scenarios genuinely imaginative, or did they cluster around "safe" risks?
- Did anyone name a failure scenario involving leadership or power dynamics? If not, why not?
- Are the mitigations real commitments, or performative planning?

## Part Seven: Facilitation Techniques

### 7.1 Managing Airtime

**Unequal participation is the norm.** Active management required:

**Techniques:**
1. **Round-robin:** Everyone speaks in order, no one twice until all have spoken once
2. **Hand signals:** Raise hand to speak, facilitator maintains queue
3. **Talking stick:** Physical object; only holder can speak
4. **Direct invitation:** "Person X, we haven't heard from you. What do you think?"
5. **Limit verbose speakers:** "Thank you. Let's hear from others before you add more"

### 7.2 Managing Power Dynamics

**Hierarchies distort dialogue.** Strategies:

1. **Name the elephant:**
   - "I know there are power differences here. Let's be explicit about how that might affect what we say"

2. **Junior speaks first:**
   - In hierarchical groups, have least powerful speak before most powerful (prevents conformity)

3. **Anonymous input:**
   - Use written submissions, then discuss without attribution

4. **Separate meetings:**
   - If hierarchy is too steep, meet with groups separately, then synthesize

### 7.3 Managing Conflict

**Conflict is normal and often productive.** When destructive:

**De-escalation techniques:**
1. **Pause and breathe:** "Let's take a 5-minute break"
2. **Name the pattern:** "I'm noticing we're talking over each other. Let's slow down"
3. **Return to ground rules:** "Remember, we're challenging ideas, not people"
4. **Separate people from problem:** "We're both trying to solve X. We just disagree on how"
5. **Acknowledge emotion:** "I can see this is bringing up strong feelings. Let's make space for that"

**When to stop:**
- Physical threat or aggression
- Participant asks to stop
- Facilitator assesses psychological harm is occurring
- Productive dialogue is impossible (escalation spiral)

**Exit strategy:**
- "We're not able to continue productively right now. Let's pause and revisit [timeline]"
- Offer individual follow-up if needed

## Part Eight: Common Pitfalls

### 8.1 Facilitator Taking Sides

**Problem:** Facilitator loses neutrality, becomes advocate for one perspective

**Solution:**
- Facilitator explicitly states neutrality at start
- If facilitator has stake in outcome, co-facilitate with neutral party
- Facilitator monitors own reactions, catches bias early

### 8.2 Groupthink

**Problem:** Group converges on answer too quickly, suppressing dissent

**Solution:**
- Assign "devil's advocate" role
- Explicitly ask: "Who sees this differently?"
- Use 1-2-4-All to surface individual views before group

### 8.3 Performative Participation

**Problem:** Participants say what they think they should say, not what they actually think

**Solution:**
- Build psychological safety over time (don't expect depth immediately)
- Anonymous input first, then discuss
- Model vulnerability (facilitator or leader goes first with honesty)

### 8.4 Analysis Paralysis

**Problem:** Group interrogates endlessly without reaching conclusions or actions

**Solution:**
- Set time limits for each phase
- Move from exploration to synthesis explicitly ("We've explored. Now let's synthesize.")
- Document "unresolved questions" and move forward anyway

### 8.5 Unequal Emotional Labor

**Problem:** Same people always do the work of naming tensions, facilitating hard conversations

**Solution:**
- Rotate facilitation
- Explicitly acknowledge and thank emotional labor
- Build capacity across group so burden is shared

## Part Nine: Session Structure Templates

### Template 1: Team Debrief (60 minutes)

**Purpose:** Extract lessons from completed project

**Structure:**
1. **Opening (5 min):**
   - Ground rules review
   - Agenda overview

2. **What happened? (10 min):**
   - Each person shares key moments (facts only)
   - Scribe captures on board

3. **How did it feel? (10 min):**
   - Observation voice: energy, dynamics, tone
   - "The room felt..." "The team was..."

4. **Why did it unfold that way? (20 min):**
   - Interpretation: What were the forces at play?
   - Multiple perspectives welcome
   - Identify patterns

5. **What will we do differently? (10 min):**
   - Actionable lessons
   - Who owns each action?

6. **Closing (5 min):**
   - Appreciations
   - Final reflections

### Template 2: Conflict Mediation (90 minutes)

**Purpose:** Resolve or understand conflict between parties

**Structure:**
1. **Opening (10 min):**
   - Ground rules (especially confidentiality, no interruptions)
   - Each party commits to seeking understanding

2. **Party A shares (15 min):**
   - Party A: "Here's my experience"
   - Party B: Loops back (confirms understanding)
   - Facilitator ensures Party A feels heard

3. **Party B shares (15 min):**
   - Party B: "Here's my experience"
   - Party A: Loops back
   - Facilitator ensures Party B feels heard

4. **Identify shared interests (15 min):**
   - What do both parties need?
   - Where are interests compatible?

5. **Problem-solving (25 min):**
   - Brainstorm solutions
   - Test against both parties' needs
   - Agree on way forward (or agree to disagree)

6. **Closing (10 min):**
   - Commitments made explicit
   - Follow-up plan
   - Appreciations

### Template 3: Pre-Mortem (45 minutes)

**Purpose:** Surface risks before starting project

**Structure:**
1. **Setup (5 min):**
   - "Imagine it's 6 months from now. This project failed. Why?"

2. **Individual brainstorm (5 min):**
   - Silent writing: failure scenarios

3. **Share in pairs (10 min):**
   - Compare scenarios, identify patterns

4. **Full group synthesis (15 min):**
   - Each pair shares top 2-3 risks
   - Facilitator clusters similar risks

5. **Mitigation planning (10 min):**
   - For top 3-5 risks: How prevent/mitigate?
   - Assign owners

## Appendix: Collaborative Interrogation Quick Reference

**Method 1: Collective Descent**
- What happened? (facts) > How? (tone, dynamics) > Why? (interpretations)
- Ensure all voices contribute at each layer

**Method 2: Multiple Perspectives**
- Individual views first (prevent conformity) > Map overlaps/divergences > Synthesize or document disagreement
- Divergences reveal blind spots, not errors

**Method 3: The Fishbowl**
- Inner circle dialogues, outer circle observes > Full group debrief
- Use for large groups or modeling interrogation

**Method 4: Rotating Facilitator**
- Each person facilitates and is interrogated > Debrief patterns
- Distributes power, builds skill

**Method 5: The Looping**
- Person A speaks > Person B paraphrases > Confirm or correct > Switch > Only then problem-solve
- Most conflicts escalate because people don't feel heard

**Method 6: Ladder of Inference**
- Facts > Selected data > Interpretation > Assumptions > Conclusions > Actions
- Map both ladders side by side; find where they diverged

**Method 7: Underlying Interests**
- Positions (what you want) > Interests (why you want it) > Core needs > Shared interests > Creative solutions
- Positions conflict; needs often align

**Method 8: After-Action Review**
- What was supposed to happen? > What actually happened? > Why the difference? > What next?
- Blame-free zone is non-negotiable

**Method 9: 1-2-4-All**
- 1 min silent > 2 min pairs > 4 min quartets > All share
- Ensures introverts contribute before extroverts dominate

**Method 10: Pre-Mortem**
- Imagine catastrophic failure > Brainstorm causes > Identify patterns > Mitigate
- Permission to be "negative" without penalty

**When you're short on time, ask these three questions:**
1. What are we all assuming that we haven't tested?
2. Whose perspective is missing from this room?
3. What would we need to see to know we're wrong?

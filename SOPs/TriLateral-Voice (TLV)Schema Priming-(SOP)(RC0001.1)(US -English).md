## Executive Summary

The **Methods for Schema Priming** is a Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) for systematically constructing a connected evidence framework — a schema — before beginning any sustained learning, analysis, research, or investigative task.

**Core Principle:** The brain processes new information by doing two things simultaneously: understanding WHAT something is, and figuring out HOW IT FITS with existing knowledge. When forced to do both at once, processing suffers, connections are missed, and information is lost. Schema priming separates these tasks — build the framework first, then process detail within the framework.[^1]

**Why this matters:**

Without a pre-built schema, practitioners constantly:
- Process new information without knowing where it fits — increasing cognitive load and reducing retention[^2][^1]
- Miss connections between sources that would be visible with a map of the terrain
- Duplicate effort by re-locating sources they encountered but didn't register
- Build conclusions on incomplete evidence because they didn't know what existed
- Produce work with citation gaps that become visible only under scrutiny
- Fill structural gaps with assumptions — projecting plausibility where evidence should be[^3]

By priming the schema before sustained work begins, practitioners:
- Scope the entire landscape of available information before committing to a path[^1]
- Build a connected framework that makes every subsequent task faster and more accurate[^2]
- Know where to look for specific types of evidence before they need it[^1]
- Form testable hypotheses that give the brain a "head start" on how to think about the material[^1]
- Flag gaps and contradictions upfront so they can be investigated, not papered over
- Create a living resource directory that prevents orphaned claims and enables full provenance tracing

***

## Mission

To provide practitioners with systematic methods for constructing a connected evidence framework — a schema — before beginning sustained analytical, research, or investigative work, enabling faster processing, fewer missed connections, complete source tracking, and an auditable chain of evidence throughout any TLV workflow.

## Core Understanding

The [core Interrogation of Thought SOP](./Methods-for-the-Interrogation-of-Thought-SOP-RC0001.1-US-English.md) addresses the problem of unexamined assumptions — the "white marble" problem where projection is mistaken for observation. The [Interrogation Methods SOP](./Interrogation-Methods-SOP-RC0001.1-US-English.md) provides micro-techniques for surfacing depth beneath surface communication. The [Self-Interrogation SOP](./Methods-for-Self-Interrogation-of-Thought-SOP-RC0001.1-US-English.md) turns those tools inward. The [Collaborative Interrogation SOP](./Methods-for-Collaborative-Interrogation-of-Thought-SOP-RC0001.1-US-English.md) scales them to groups.[^4][^5][^6][^3]

This SOP addresses a different but equally fundamental problem: **the empty map problem.**

The white marble problem is about projecting meaning onto incomplete information. The empty map problem is about *not having a map at all*. When a practitioner begins sustained work — a biography, a transcription, an interpretation, a takedown analysis, a research project — without first scoping the terrain, they are walking into unknown territory without a map. They will find things. They will miss things. They won't know which is which until it's too late.

This is the cognitive parallel to the white marble problem. Just as scholars projected aesthetic values onto unpainted statues because they didn't know the paint existed, practitioners project completeness onto their evidence base because they don't know what they haven't found. The assumption is invisible: *"I've looked at enough. What I have is sufficient. The gaps aren't important."* That assumption is almost always wrong. And in high-stakes work — legal defense, court-ready analysis, published investigations — it's the assumption that gets you destroyed under cross-examination.[^3]

Schema theory, originating with Frederic Bartlett in 1932 and developed by Jean Piaget, establishes that memory is not a warehouse of separate items but a web of interconnected mental structures — schemas — that organize experience and guide interpretation of new information. Bartlett's key insight was that memory is an "imaginative reconstruction" based on these schemas — not playback, but active rebuilding shaped by what the brain already knows. When schemas are well-developed, new information integrates easily. When they are absent or underdeveloped, new information either gets distorted to fit existing (wrong) frameworks, or it falls away entirely.[^7][^8][^9][^10][^2]

Schema priming applies this science operationally: **build the schema deliberately, upfront, before the brain has to process detail**. The schema becomes the map. The map prevents both the white marble problem (projecting meaning onto what's there) and the empty map problem (not knowing what's not there).[^1]

**Schema priming is not deep analysis.** It is fast, wide, and deliberately shallow. The depth comes later. The schema is the scaffolding that makes depth possible.[^1]

## Part One: Why Schema Priming Matters

### 1.1 The Cognitive Problem: Dual-Processing Overload

When the brain encounters new information, it must accomplish two tasks simultaneously:[^1]

1. **Understand what the information IS** (semantic processing — what does this mean?)
2. **Determine where it FITS** (schematic processing — how does this connect to what's already known?)

Both tasks compete for the same cognitive resources. When a practitioner encounters a new source, a new claim, a new contradiction — the brain is simultaneously trying to comprehend it AND figure out where it belongs in the larger picture. This dual load causes predictable failures:[^1]

- **Pruning:** The brain aggressively discards information it can't immediately place — not because it's unimportant, but because there's no framework to hold it
- **Shallow encoding:** Information gets processed at surface level because the brain can't spare resources for deep integration
- **Missed connections:** Relationships between sources that would be obvious with a map of the terrain are invisible when processing sources one at a time
- **False completion:** The brain fills structural gaps with assumptions, creating the illusion of a complete picture

### 1.2 The Schema Solution

A schema — in cognitive science terms — is a connected web of concepts and their relationships that exists in long-term memory. Schemas serve as mental templates derived from prior experience that guide attention, perception, memory, and learning. Well-developed schemas make new information processing dramatically more efficient because the "where it fits" question is already largely answered.[^8][^9][^10][^2]

Schema priming deliberately constructs this web BEFORE the detailed work begins. The result:[^1]

- The brain only has to handle ONE task when processing detail — understanding what it IS — because the framework for WHERE IT FITS already exists
- Connections between sources are visible from the start because the map shows the terrain
- Gaps are identifiable because the map shows what should be there but isn't
- The practitioner knows WHERE TO LOOK for specific types of evidence because source categories have been scoped[^1]

### 1.3 The TLV Application: Schema as Evidentiary Infrastructure

In TLV practice, schema priming serves a specific additional function: **it creates the auditable evidence infrastructure that prevents hallucination and enables citation tracing.**

Every TLV protocol — from Biography (BIO) to Interpretation (INTERP) to Conflict Mediation (CMED) — produces claims. Those claims must trace to sources. Without a schema, source tracking is distributed across phases, ad hoc, and prone to gaps. With a schema, every source is registered, every claim is linked, and every gap is documented — from the beginning.

The schema becomes the **Resource Directory** — a living document that every subsequent phase of any TLV workflow reads from and writes to. Nothing enters the analytical pipeline without being registered. Nothing exits the pipeline without a provenance chain.

**Interrogation Checkpoint (SOP-IOT Phase 1):**
- Am I clear about what I'm about to analyze?
- Do I know the scope of available information, or am I assuming?
- What happens to my conclusions if key sources exist that I haven't found?

## Part Two: The Three Steps of Schema Priming

Schema priming follows three steps, adapted from the methodology developed by Dr. Justin Sung. Each step has a specific cognitive function. Together, they produce a usable schema in a constrained time window.[^1]

**Time constraint:** Schema priming should be completed in a bounded time appropriate to the task complexity. For a single-topic learning task, 10 minutes may suffice. For a complex investigative project, 2–4 hours is appropriate. The key principle: **schema priming is fast and wide by design.** If it takes longer than the bounded time, the practitioner is going too deep. Depth comes in the subsequent analytical work — the schema is the scaffolding, not the building.[^1]

### Step 1: Scope the Topic

**Cognitive function:** Establish the lay of the land — identify what exists before trying to understand any of it.

**Principle:** It is extremely difficult to organize information when you don't know what you're organizing. Schema priming starts with scoping — surveying all available sources broadly and quickly to identify the main pillars of the topic.[^1]

**Process:**

1. **Identify all source categories** relevant to the task (see Method 1: Syntopical Scoping below)
2. **Rapidly survey** each category — not to read in depth, but to register what exists
3. **Extract main ideas only** — the pillars, the key claims, the structural elements
4. **Build basic scaffolding** — a rough framework of how the major elements relate

**What "scoping" looks like:**
- Scanning tables of contents, headings, subheadings, key terms
- Reading opening and closing paragraphs of sources (not middles)
- Noting who the key voices are and what positions they hold
- Registering the types of sources available (primary records, secondary commentary, the target's own content, critical voices, etc.)

**What scoping is NOT:**
- It is not reading. It is not analysis. It is not note-taking for comprehension.
- If you are forming opinions about the content, you are going too deep.
- If you are writing detailed notes, you are going too deep.
- If you are spending more than 2–3 minutes per source, you are going too deep.

**Interrogation Checkpoint (SOP-IOT Phase 4 — Contextualize):**
- Am I scoping broadly enough? Have I checked all source categories?
- Am I going too deep? Am I analyzing when I should be mapping?
- What source categories might exist that I haven't thought of?

### Step 2: Make Judgments and Hypotheses

**Cognitive function:** Begin connecting the ideas collected during scoping — forming the actual schema by comparing, contrasting, and building preliminary relationships.[^1]

**Principle:** Collecting ideas is not the same as forming a schema. The schema is the web of connections between ideas — and that web must be actively constructed. The goal is to form hypotheses about HOW TO THINK about the material, not to determine what to remember.[^1]

**Process:**

1. **Compare and contrast** the main ideas identified during scoping
2. **Judge relative importance** — which connections seem most significant?
3. **Form hypotheses** — preliminary statements about what the evidence will show
4. **Map relationships** — supports, contradicts, contextualizes, depends on

**Critical distinction:** These are explicitly **hypotheses**, not conclusions. The schema is a starting framework that will be refined, confirmed, refuted, and updated throughout the analytical work. Treating hypotheses as conclusions is the projection problem the Interrogation SOP warns against.[^3]

**What hypothesis formation looks like:**
- "Source A and Source C appear to contradict each other on [topic] — investigate in Phase [X]"
- "The target's claimed credentials are prominent in their own content but absent from independent sources — verify"
- "Three independent sources point to a pattern of [behavior] — confirm or refute with primary evidence"
- "The strongest counter-argument appears to be [X] — address directly"

**What hypothesis formation is NOT:**
- It is not conclusion-drawing. It is not judging the target. It is not forming opinions.
- If you feel certain about your hypotheses, you're treating them as conclusions. Back up.
- If your hypotheses all point in the same direction, you may be projecting. Apply SOP-IOT Phase 2 (Map Assumptions).

**Interrogation Checkpoint (SOP-IOT Phase 2 — Map Assumptions):**
- What assumptions am I embedding in these hypotheses?
- Are my hypotheses genuinely testable, or are they disguised conclusions?
- Do any of my hypotheses reflect what I WANT to find rather than what the evidence suggests?
- Have I formed at least one hypothesis I hope is wrong? (If not, bias is likely.)

### Step 3: Prep Your Future Self

**Cognitive function:** Set flags on complicated areas, actively identify problems and unknowns, and create targeted questions that will guide focused investigation in subsequent work.[^1]

**Principle:** The most valuable output of schema priming is not what you found — it's what you flagged. Unknowns identified early become investigation targets. Unknowns discovered late become embarrassing gaps. Priming research shows that pre-testing and flagging creates "memory traces" that improve recognition of answers when they're later encountered.[^1]

**Process:**

1. **Flag complicated areas** — topics where the scoping revealed complexity, contradiction, or insufficient information
2. **Actively find problems** — things that don't make sense, claims that seem inconsistent, sources that disagree
3. **Generate targeted questions** — specific questions that subsequent analytical phases should answer
4. **Assign investigation targets** — which phase/method/source should resolve which question
5. **Note access gaps** — sources that exist but require specific steps to obtain (court records, paywalled research, foreign-language material, interview requests)

**What prepping looks like:**
- "Target claims [X credential] but no independent verification found — Flag: resolve in Phase 1 biographical research"
- "Sources A and B disagree on [fact] — Flag: determine which is accurate, primary source required"
- "Target's early career has minimal documentation — Flag: look for [specific source types]"
- "Phase 6 (Translation Test) will require cultural expertise in [culture] — Flag: identify consultant"

**Interrogation Checkpoint (SOP-IOT Phase 3 — Test Falsifiability):**
- Have I flagged enough gaps? If everything looks clean, I may not have scoped broadly enough.
- What evidence would DISPROVE my hypotheses? Have I flagged where to find it?
- Am I flagging problems honestly, or avoiding uncomfortable unknowns?

## Part Three: Schema Priming Methods

The three steps above define WHAT to do. The methods below define HOW to do it — the specific techniques for each step. These methods can be applied individually or in combination depending on the task.

### Method 1: Syntopical Scoping

**Purpose:** Survey all available sources simultaneously — not one at a time — to build a panoramic view of the evidence landscape.[^1]

**Origin:** Syntopical reading, as defined by Mortimer Adler in *How to Read a Book*, is the highest level of reading — reading multiple sources on the same subject and placing them in relation to one another. The syntopical reader does not serve the books; the books serve the reader's investigation. Schema priming adapts this principle: during scoping, the practitioner is not trying to understand any single source deeply. They are surveying ALL sources to understand the landscape.[^11][^1]

**Process:**

1. **Identify source categories.** Before touching any individual source, list the TYPES of sources that should exist for this topic. Categories vary by task but commonly include:

| Category | Description | Typical Reliability |
|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Primary records** | Court filings, business registrations, academic transcripts, official documents | Highest |
| **Target's own content** | Books, videos, social media, courses, interviews, website | High for claims; assess for self-serving framing |
| **Target's commercial ecosystem** | Products, pricing, affiliate structures, business entities | High for follow-the-money analysis |
| **Verified journalism** | Published reporting from credible outlets | Moderate-high; assess for editorial perspective |
| **Critical voices** | Former followers, competitors, debunkers, legal actions against target | Moderate; assess for bias |
| **Supportive voices** | Testimonials, fan communities, affiliate promoters | Low-moderate; assess for incentive structures |
| **Academic/expert context** | Peer-reviewed research in target's claimed domain | Highest for domain claims |
| **Cultural/industry context** | Norms, comparable figures, market analysis | Moderate; provides baseline |

2. **Rapid survey within each category.** Spend 2–3 minutes maximum per source. Extract ONLY: what this source covers, what type of evidence it provides, and any immediately visible red flags or contradictions.

3. **Register each source.** Every source gets a unique identifier (SE-ID) and a brief registration entry in the Resource Directory (see Part Four).

**When to use:** ALWAYS. Syntopical scoping is the foundation of every schema prime. No task is simple enough to skip it.

**Interrogation Checkpoint:**
- Have I checked ALL source categories, or only the ones that come easily?
- Am I spending too long on individual sources? (If more than 3 minutes, pull back.)
- What source category am I most tempted to skip? (That's probably where the most important evidence lives.)

***

### Method 2: Smart Skimming

**Purpose:** Extract structural information from sources efficiently — focusing on the architecture of information rather than its content.[^1]

**Process:**

1. **Target structural elements:** headings, subheadings, tables of contents, abstracts, executive summaries, chapter titles, section breaks, bolded terms, diagrams, lists, conclusions
2. **Extract main claims:** What is this source ABOUT? What is its primary assertion?
3. **Note the framing:** How does this source position itself? As objective? Advocacy? Defense? Attack? Neutral?
4. **Register the detail level:** Does this source provide granular evidence (timestamps, quotes, documents) or surface-level commentary?
5. **Move on.** The source has been registered. Its detail will be accessed later when a specific phase needs it.

**The "10-Second Test":** For any source, you should be able to answer these questions within 10 seconds of smart skimming:
- What TYPE of source is this? (primary record, commentary, target's own content, etc.)
- What TOPIC does it cover? (biography, specific claim, financial, legal, cultural)
- What DETAIL LEVEL does it provide? (granular evidence, moderate analysis, surface opinion)
- Is it USEFUL for this investigation? (yes/probably/maybe/no)

**When to use:** Within every syntopical scoping pass. Smart skimming is the micro-technique that makes syntopical scoping fast.

**Interrogation Checkpoint:**
- Am I actually skimming, or am I reading? (If I'm forming interpretations, I'm reading.)
- Can I answer the 10-Second Test for this source? If not, I haven't extracted what I need.
- Am I registering sources I find uncomfortable or inconvenient, or only ones that support my emerging narrative?

***

### Method 3: Detail Coding

**Purpose:** Classify what TYPE of evidence each source provides so the practitioner knows WHERE TO LOOK when specific evidence is needed later.[^1]

**Principle:** Different sources provide different types of information at different levels of depth. A court filing provides legal facts but not emotional context. A YouTube video provides delivery and claims but not independent verification. Knowing which source provides which type of detail before it's needed saves enormous time and prevents the common failure of searching every source for every answer.[^1]

**Process:**

For each registered source, classify:

| Dimension | Question | Example Codes |
|-----------|----------|--------------|
| **Evidence type** | What KIND of evidence does this provide? | Factual/Testimonial/Analytical/Promotional/Legal/Financial/Emotional |
| **Detail level** | How DEEP does this evidence go? | Granular (timestamps, quotes, documents) / Moderate (summaries, analysis) / Surface (opinions, impressions) |
| **Best for** | What QUESTION is this source best at answering? | "What they said" / "What they did" / "What happened legally" / "How audiences responded" / "What experts say" |
| **Not useful for** | What should I NOT come to this source for? | "Independent verification" / "Emotional context" / "Legal facts" |
| **Access difficulty** | How HARD is it to access this source fully? | Open/Paywalled/Requires FOIA/Requires interview/Foreign language |

**The Detail Code Map:** The compiled detail codes form a map — a quick-reference table that any phase of the pipeline can consult to find the right source for the right question without searching.

**When to use:** During and immediately after syntopical scoping. Detail coding is what transforms a list of sources into a navigable resource.

**Interrogation Checkpoint:**
- Have I coded for what sources are NOT useful for? (This prevents misuse.)
- Am I honest about access difficulty? (Pretending a source is accessible when it requires significant effort to obtain creates false confidence.)
- Is there a type of evidence I need that NO source provides? (That's a gap. Flag it.)

***

### Method 4: Hypothesis Mapping

**Purpose:** Actively construct the schema by forming and mapping preliminary connections between the ideas identified during scoping.[^1]

**Principle:** A list of sources is not a schema. A schema is a CONNECTED WEB — ideas linked by relationships. Hypothesis mapping constructs that web by making preliminary judgments about how ideas relate to each other and recording those judgments as testable hypotheses.[^9][^2]

**Process:**

1. **Identify the main pillars** — the 3–7 core topics/claims/themes that emerged from scoping
2. **For each pair of pillars, ask:** How do these relate? Do they support, contradict, depend on, or contextualize each other?
3. **Form hypotheses** about each relationship — stated as testable propositions (see Step 2 above)
4. **Map the connections** visually or in structured notation
5. **Assign investigation targets** — which subsequent analytical phase will test each hypothesis

**Connection types to map:**

| Connection Type | Symbol | Example |
|----------------|--------|---------|
| **Supports** | → | "Target's credential claim → independent verification (if found)" |
| **Contradicts** | ⊗ | "Target's stated timeline ⊗ public record timeline" |
| **Contextualizes** | ↔ | "Industry norms ↔ target's practices" |
| **Depends on** | ⊃ | "Target's authority claim ⊃ credential claim (if credential falls, authority falls)" |
| **Unexplored** | ? | "Relationship between [X] and [Y] unclear — investigate" |

**When to use:** After completing syntopical scoping and detail coding. Hypothesis mapping is what transforms a catalog of sources into a schema.

**Interrogation Checkpoint (SOP-IOT Phase 5 — Generate Alternatives):**
- Have I formed at least 3 hypotheses? (Fewer suggests shallow scoping.)
- Is there at least one hypothesis that would exonerate or support the target? (If not, the schema is biased.)
- Am I mapping connections I can see, or connections I want to see?
- What is the strongest counter-hypothesis to my emerging picture? Have I mapped it?

***

### Method 5: Gap Flagging

**Purpose:** Actively identify and register what is MISSING from the evidence landscape — unknowns, contradictions, access barriers, and competence limits.[^1]

**Principle:** Gaps identified early are investigation targets. Gaps discovered late are evidentiary failures. The most valuable function of schema priming is not confirming what exists — it's revealing what doesn't. Pre-testing and flagging creates cognitive "memory traces" that prime the brain to notice relevant information when it appears later in the analytical process.[^1]

**Process:**

1. **Evidence gaps:** Information that should exist but wasn't found during scoping
2. **Source gaps:** Entire source categories that are empty or inaccessible
3. **Contradiction gaps:** Two or more sources that disagree on verifiable facts
4. **Access gaps:** Sources that exist but require specific steps to obtain
5. **Competence gaps:** Areas requiring expertise the practitioner doesn't have (cultural, linguistic, legal, domain-specific)
6. **Problem flags:** Things that simply don't make sense — claims that seem internally inconsistent, timelines that don't add up, credentials that can't be verified

**Gap registration format:**

```
Gap-ID: [unique identifier]
Type: [evidence / source / contradiction / access / competence / problem]
Description: [what's missing or inconsistent]
Impact: [how this gap affects the analysis if unresolved]
Investigation: [who/what/when should resolve this]
Status: OPEN
```

**When to use:** Throughout schema priming, but especially at the end as a final sweep. The question is not "Did I find enough?" but "What am I missing?"

**Interrogation Checkpoint (SOP-IOT Phase 3 — Test Falsifiability):**
- Are there at least 3 gaps flagged? (Fewer suggests overconfidence or shallow scoping. There are ALWAYS gaps.)
- Am I flagging gaps that are uncomfortable? (The most dangerous gaps are the ones the practitioner is motivated to ignore.)
- What evidence would DESTROY my emerging picture? Have I flagged where to look for it?

***

### Method 6: Schema Assembly

**Purpose:** Compile the outputs of Methods 1–5 into a structured, navigable Resource Directory — the deliverable of schema priming.

**Process:**

1. **Compile the Source Registry** (from Method 1: Syntopical Scoping)
2. **Compile the Detail Code Map** (from Method 3: Detail Coding)
3. **Compile the Hypothesis Map** (from Method 4: Hypothesis Mapping)
4. **Compile the Gap Registry** (from Method 5: Gap Flagging)
5. **Extract preliminary claims** from smart skimming (Method 2) into a Claim Registry
6. **Initialize the Phase Update Log** — a chronological record of all future Schema modifications
7. **Run the Assembly Quality Check** (see below)

**Assembly Quality Check:**

- Does the Source Registry cover all relevant source categories?
- Does the Detail Code Map enable efficient source lookup by evidence type?
- Does the Hypothesis Map contain at least 3 testable hypotheses?
- Does the Gap Registry contain at least 3 open gaps?
- Can the practitioner articulate the "big picture" in 2–3 sentences? (If yes, the schema is primed. If no, scope more broadly.)
- Does the schema feel like a MAP (broad, connected, navigable) or a DEEP DIVE (narrow, detailed, analytical)? It should feel like a map. If it feels like a deep dive, pull back.[^1]

**When to use:** At the conclusion of every schema priming session, before beginning the subsequent analytical work.

**Interrogation Checkpoint:**
- Is this Resource Directory something a colleague could pick up and navigate without additional context?
- Does it honestly represent what is known, what is suspected, and what is unknown?
- Would it survive legal scrutiny as a transparent record of what was available at the start of the investigation?

## Part Four: Output — The Resource Directory

### 4.1 What the Resource Directory Is

The Resource Directory is the formal output of schema priming — a structured, living document that serves as the single source of truth for any subsequent analytical work. It is not a bibliography. It is not a reading list. It is a **connected evidence map** with provenance tracking, hypothesis management, and gap documentation.

### 4.2 Structure

```
RESOURCE DIRECTORY (Type S)
│
├── S.1: Source Registry
│   [Every source: SE-ID, type, location, access date, preservation
│    status, reliability tier, detail level, content scope]
│
├── S.2: Claim Registry
│   [Every registered claim: Claim-ID, source SE-ID, verbatim text or
│    paraphrase (marked), location in source, verification status]
│
├── S.3: Detail Code Map
│   [Source-by-source classification: evidence type, detail level,
│    best for, not useful for, access difficulty]
│
├── S.4: Connection Map
│   [Hypothesis map: Claim-ID ↔ Claim-ID, relationship type,
│    confidence level]
│
├── S.5: Hypothesis Tracker
│   [Every hypothesis: H-ID, statement, supporting evidence,
│    contradicting evidence, status, investigation assignment]
│
├── S.6: Gap Registry
│   [Every gap: Gap-ID, type, description, impact, investigation
│    assignment, status]
│
└── S.7: Phase Update Log
    [Chronological record of all modifications by phase/date/practitioner]
```

### 4.3 The Living Document Principle

Unlike most SOP outputs, the Resource Directory is a **living document.** It is constructed during schema priming and updated continuously throughout all subsequent work. Every analytical phase reads from the Schema and writes back to it:

**READ obligations (every phase):**
- Consult the Source Registry and Detail Code Map before beginning investigation
- Check hypothesis statuses before forming new conclusions
- Check gap statuses before beginning investigation

**WRITE obligations (every phase):**
- Register any new sources discovered (new SE-IDs)
- Register any new claims extracted (new Claim-IDs linked to SE-IDs)
- Update hypothesis statuses when evidence confirms, refutes, or modifies
- Update gap statuses when gaps are resolved or new gaps discovered
- Log all modifications in the Phase Update Log

### 4.4 The Schema Rule

**If a factual claim appears in any output document and cannot be traced to a registered entry in the Resource Directory, it must be either:**

1. **Registered immediately** — source identified, SE-ID created, Claim-ID linked
2. **Removed** — if no source can be identified, the claim does not belong in the output

There is no third option. Analytical conclusions and interpretations are clearly labeled as such (per the Interrogation SOP's observation/interpretation distinction). But factual claims must trace to registered sources. The Resource Directory is where that trace lives.[^3]

## Part Five: Integration with TLV Protocols and SOPs

### 5.1 Relationship to Existing SOPs

Schema Priming (SOP-SP) operates as a **pre-process** for all other SOPs and protocols. It does not replace any existing methodology — it provides the evidence infrastructure that makes every methodology more effective.

| SOP/Protocol | How SOP-SP Integrates |
|---|---|
| **SOP-IOT** (Interrogation of Thought) | SOP-SP provides the evidence base against which SOP-IOT's seven phases test assumptions. Phase 2 (Map Assumptions) is more rigorous when the Schema shows what evidence exists. Phase 3 (Test Falsifiability) is more rigorous when gaps have been flagged. Phase 5 (Generate Alternatives) is more rigorous when the full landscape is mapped.[^3] |
| **SOP-IM** (Interrogation Methods) | SOP-SP's Detail Code Map tells practitioners WHERE to apply SOP-IM's micro-methods. The Descent, The Contradiction, The Reframe, The Pattern, The Counterfactual, and The Embodiment all benefit from knowing what sources are available and what they contain.[^6] |
| **SOP-SI** (Self-Interrogation) | SOP-SP can be applied to self-knowledge: "What do I know about myself? What sources of self-knowledge exist? What gaps?" The Schema Rule applied to self-interrogation: if a self-belief can't be traced to experience, it may be inherited assumption — exactly what SOP-SI's Timeline method investigates.[^5] |
| **SOP-CI** (Collaborative Interrogation) | For team-based analysis, SOP-SP is constructed collaboratively — different team members scope different source categories, then merge into a unified Schema. SOP-CI Method 9 (1-2-4-All) can be applied to hypothesis formation. SOP-CI Method 10 (Pre-Mortem) integrates directly with Gap Flagging.[^4] |

### 5.2 Relationship to TLV Protocols

| Protocol | How SOP-SP Integrates |
|---|---|

### 5.3 Specific Integration: Takedown Workflow

In the TLV Takedown Workflow, Schema Priming operates as **Phase 0** — the mandatory pre-pipeline step that constructs the evidence infrastructure before the seven-phase analytical pipeline begins. The Resource Directory becomes the single source of truth for the entire Takedown, enabling:

- **Three-link citation chain:** Any claim in any document type (A–J) traces → Claim-ID in Schema → SE-ID in Schema → original source with full provenance
- **Anti-hallucination guarantee:** The Schema Rule ensures no orphaned claims survive to final output
- **Legal discovery readiness:** The Resource Directory is the first document produced in litigation — showing exactly what was known, when, and from where

### 5.4 Standalone Use

Schema Priming is not limited to TLV workflows. The SOP applies to any task that involves sustained engagement with complex information:

- **Academic research:** Scope the literature before deep reading
- **Investigative journalism:** Map the source landscape before interviews
- **Legal case preparation:** Build the evidence directory before analysis
- **Business due diligence:** Scope all available information before investment decisions
- **Learning a new subject:** Build the schema before studying detail[^1]
- **Policy analysis:** Map stakeholder positions and evidence before drafting
- **Creative projects requiring research:** Scope reference material before production

In standalone use, the Resource Directory may be simplified — not every section is necessary for every task. But the principle is universal: **map the terrain before you walk it.**

## Part Six: Common Pitfalls

### Pitfall 1: Skipping Schema Priming Entirely

"I already know this topic — I don't need to scope." This is the most dangerous pitfall. The practitioner's existing knowledge is unregistered, uncited, and unauditable. The schema must be built even when the practitioner thinks they know the landscape. Existing knowledge gets REGISTERED in the Schema — it doesn't bypass it.

**Fix:** Schema priming is non-negotiable for any high-stakes work. For low-stakes work, a 10-minute mini-prime is always worth the investment.

### Pitfall 2: Going Too Deep

The practitioner spends hours on Phase 0, building an exhaustive schema and never getting to the actual analytical work. This is the depth trap that schema priming specifically warns against — the scoping phase should be fast and wide, not slow and deep.[^1]

**Fix:** Enforce the time boundary. If the schema doesn't feel complete at the boundary, log remaining gaps and proceed. The schema is a living document — it gets updated throughout the pipeline.

### Pitfall 3: Treating the Schema as Static

The practitioner builds the schema and never updates it. By Phase 4, half the claims reference sources that aren't registered because they were discovered during Phases 1–3. The citation chain breaks.

**Fix:** The Phase Update Log (S.7) exists to make update compliance auditable. Every phase writes to the Schema.

### Pitfall 4: Schema as Confirmation Tool

The practitioner forms hypotheses and then uses the Schema to confirm rather than test them. The Hypothesis Tracker becomes a scorecard instead of an investigation tool.

**Fix:** Apply SOP-IOT Phase 2 during hypothesis formation. Require at least one hypothesis that the practitioner hopes is wrong. At synthesis, review: if every hypothesis was confirmed, the testing may not have been genuine.

### Pitfall 5: Over-Indexing on Registered Sources

The practitioner only analyzes what's in the Schema, ignoring relevant evidence that emerges organically during later work because "it's not registered."

**Fix:** New sources discovered in any phase are registered immediately via the Phase Update Log. The Schema grows. It never constrains.

### Pitfall 6: Beautiful Schema, No Analysis

The practitioner falls in love with the schema-building process — creating an elegant, color-coded, meticulously organized Resource Directory — and treats it as the product rather than the scaffold.

**Fix:** Remember the cognitive science: schema priming works because it separates framework-building from detail-processing. Once the framework is built, MOVE TO THE DETAIL WORK. The schema's value is measured by what it enables, not by its own elegance.[^1]

### Pitfall 7: Schema as Surveillance

In investigative contexts, the scoping power of schema priming can surface vast amounts of information about a target — including material tangential to the investigation. The temptation is to register everything "just in case."

**Fix:** Register what's relevant. Note what exists but isn't relevant. Do not weaponize tangential information simply because the Schema made it easy to find. The Schema should reflect the investigation's scope, not the practitioner's curiosity.

## Part Seven: Example Sessions

### Example 1: Schema Priming for a TLV Takedown (Complex, 2–4 hours)

**Task:** Prepare to analyze a wellness influencer who sells a $5,000 coaching program.

**Step 1 — Syntopical Scoping (45–60 min):**

*Source categories surveyed:*
- Target's YouTube channel (147 videos, scanned titles/descriptions) — SE-001
- Target's website and sales pages — SE-002
- Target's Instagram (scanned highlights and recent posts) — SE-003
- Better Business Bureau complaints — SE-004 (3 complaints found)
- State licensing board — SE-005 (no active license found — FLAG)
- Published interviews (3 podcasts found) — SE-006, SE-007, SE-008
- Critical Reddit threads (2 found) — SE-009, SE-010
- Target's published book — SE-011
- Academic research on target's claimed methodology — SE-012 through SE-015

*Main pillars identified:* Credential claims, methodology claims, pricing/value proposition, testimonial patterns, audience demographics

**Step 2 — Judgments and Hypotheses (20–30 min):**

- H-001: "Target's claimed credential [specific] appears unverifiable — no independent record found during scoping" (Status: HYPOTHESIS)
- H-002: "Target's methodology appears to rebrand established therapeutic techniques without attribution" (Status: HYPOTHESIS)
- H-003: "Target's testimonials show consistent pattern — investigate for incentive structure" (Status: HYPOTHESIS)
- H-004: "Target may be operating as unlicensed practitioner — SE-005 showed no license" (Status: HYPOTHESIS)
- H-005 (counter-hypothesis): "Target may provide genuine value that explains audience loyalty despite red flags" (Status: HYPOTHESIS)

**Step 3 — Prep Your Future Self (15–20 min):**

- Gap-001: No independent credential verification found. Type: Evidence. Impact: If credential claim is false, entire authority basis collapses. Investigation: Phase 1 biographical research, contact issuing institution directly. Status: OPEN.
- Gap-002: Target's early career pre-2018 has no documentation. Type: Source. Impact: Can't establish trajectory or pattern. Investigation: Look for archived social media, LinkedIn history. Status: OPEN.
- Gap-003: No legal filings found, but BBB complaints suggest possible consumer protection issues. Type: Access. Impact: If filings exist, they're critical. Investigation: Check state AG consumer complaint database. Status: OPEN.
- Gap-004: Target publishes in Spanish-language markets — need Spanish-language source review. Type: Competence. Investigation: Identify bilingual team member or consultant. Status: OPEN.

**Output:** Type S Resource Directory with 15 registered sources, 5 hypotheses, 4 gaps, Detail Code Map covering all source categories.

**Time spent:** 2 hours 15 minutes.

### Example 2: Schema Priming for Academic Learning (Quick, 10–15 min)

**Task:** Prepare to study a new chapter on cognitive development theories.

**Step 1 — Scope (5 min):**
- Skim chapter headings, subheadings, bolded terms, diagrams
- Note the main theorists covered: Piaget, Vygotsky, Bronfenbrenner
- Note the chapter structure: historical context → theory comparison → applications → critiques
- Identify supplementary sources: 3 recommended readings at chapter end

**Step 2 — Hypotheses (3 min):**
- "Piaget and Vygotsky probably agree on development being stage-based but disagree on the role of social interaction"
- "Bronfenbrenner probably adds an environmental/systems dimension the other two lack"
- "The chapter probably argues these theories are complementary rather than competing"

**Step 3 — Prep (2 min):**
- Flag: "What does Bronfenbrenner's model actually look like? I have no schema for it — priority for careful reading"
- Flag: "How do these theories apply to adult learning, not just children? Chapter may not cover this — check supplementary readings"
- Question: "If these theories are complementary, why are they taught separately?"

**Output:** Mental schema with three theoretical pillars, preliminary connections, and two flags for focused attention.

**Time spent:** 10 minutes.

### Example 3: Schema Priming for Team Research (Collaborative, 3–4 hours)

**Task:** Team of 4 analysts preparing to investigate a political figure's financial disclosures.

**Phase 0 conducted using SOP-CI Method 9 (1-2-4-All):**[^4]

**Individual scoping (60 min):** Each analyst scopes 2 source categories:
- Analyst A: Public financial disclosures + tax records

## Appendix A: Quick Reference — Schema Priming Steps and Methods

**Step 1: Scope the Topic**
- Survey ALL source categories broadly and quickly[^1]
- Register every source encountered (SE-ID)
- Extract main ideas only — pillars, not detail
- 2–3 minutes maximum per source

**Step 2: Make Judgments and Hypotheses**
- Compare and contrast main ideas[^1]
- Form testable hypotheses (NOT conclusions)
- Map connections: supports/contradicts/contextualizes/depends on
- Include at least one counter-hypothesis

**Step 3: Prep Your Future Self**
- Flag complicated areas with targeted questions[^1]
- Actively find problems — things that don't make sense
- Assign investigation targets to subsequent phases
- Note access gaps and competence gaps

**Method 1: Syntopical Scoping** — Survey all sources simultaneously across all categories
**Method 2: Smart Skimming** — Extract structural info (headings, claims, framing, detail level)
**Method 3: Detail Coding** — Classify what each source is best/worst for
**Method 4: Hypothesis Mapping** — Form and connect testable hypotheses
**Method 5: Gap Flagging** — Identify and register what's missing
**Method 6: Schema Assembly** — Compile into structured Resource Directory

**Time Boundaries:**
- Simple learning task: 10 minutes
- Moderate research: 30–60 minutes
- Complex investigation: 2–4 hours
- Team-based analysis: 3–4 hours (parallelized)

**The Assembly Quality Check:**
- All source categories covered?
- At least 3 hypotheses formed?
- At least 3 gaps flagged?
- Can you state the "big picture" in 2–3 sentences?
- Does it feel like a MAP (good) or a DEEP DIVE (too narrow)?

## Appendix C: Ethical Considerations

**Schema Honesty:** The Resource Directory must honestly represent what was known and when. Retroactively editing the Schema to improve the appearance of the evidentiary chain — removing resolved gaps, hiding refuted hypotheses, backdating source registrations — is dishonest and, in legal contexts, potentially fraudulent. The Schema is a record of the investigation, including its wrong turns.

**Schema Proportionality:** The scoping power of schema priming can surface vast information about a subject. Register what's relevant to the investigation's scope. Note what exists but isn't relevant. Do not weaponize tangential information.

**Schema Accessibility:** When working in teams, the Schema must be accessible to all team members. Information asymmetry within a team undermines collaborative interrogation. The Schema Custodian role exists to ensure access, not to gatekeep.

**Schema and Privacy:** Schema priming for investigative purposes may surface personal information about subjects. All source registration and evidence handling must comply with applicable privacy laws and ethical standards. The Schema documents public-record and published-source information — it does not authorize invasive investigation.
